In the Beginning...

Matt,
I'm getting mighty tired of all this nonsense about the subconscious mind and how you can use it, lah di da, to write. If you had any brains you might be dangerous but I don't think so.

Sy Onara

Sy,
Oh boy, now I can breathe a sigh of relief. It's good to know there is nothing "out there" or even "in there" that will help anyone on the journey of becoming a writer, much less a published author.

It's good to know that there's nothing useful about one's imagination, other than to imagine that you don't have or use your imagination - ever.

Imagine the audacity to claim that a writer can imagine oneself to be a writer and become one.

And imagine that someone who imagines becoming a writer will block that imaginary idea by imagining that he or she can't become one, or even worse, cannot do so anymore because of A.I.

Imagine a world where no one can write anymore because no one can imagine there is such a thing as an imagination.

"In the beginning was the Word," and that, as you know, was not the end.

No creating took place after that? None at all? No one, not even God, who was the Word, imagined anything? It was all just a Big Blang?

And then along came Steven Wright, and man, could he write.

An odd man, a comedian, who in his own word says he comes up with "a word," writes it down. Just one word.

In the beginning.

Then he walks away from that word, and after doing so, that word takes wings, and when he's not even trying to think about it consciously, he feels an inner urge to give the word some flesh by fleshing it out on paper.

The beginning word grew of its own accord, effortlessly, forming other words, along with ideas and images.

Imagine that!

And imagine that Steven Wright's wrongful association of words produce laughter - lots and lots of laughter.

Mr. Wright's words come from "parts unknown" and after he writes about them in a supposedly wrong way those words create a big bang of laughter that blows the doors off the subconscious minds of those who have ears and can hear. And all of these words come from the knowable unknown.

Is it hard work to write one word then take a walk and have that word mysteriously and mystically explode into something more than that word?

I imagine not!

The one word is the beginning. And anyone can start at the beginning. And the funny thing about the beginning is that it is also the end.

That, Mr. Sy Onara, is not an idea worthy of wave goodbye to.

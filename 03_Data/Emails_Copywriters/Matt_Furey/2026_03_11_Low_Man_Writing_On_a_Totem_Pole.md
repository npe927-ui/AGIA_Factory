# Low Man Writing On a Totem Pole

In 1941, H. Allen Smith's best-seller, Low Man On a Totem Pole, was published. It became a best-seller, read around the world, including by U.S. troops fighting in World War II.

Smith dropped out of school after eighth grade, worked a number of "odd jobs" for a while, including his last one in a barbershop where a customer suggested he gain employment at a local newspaper in Indiana.

Smith was 15 when he began his "low man on the writing totem pole" position.

From this lowly beginning, Smith became a reporter for various newspaper, before landing in New York City, where he conducted interviews with the rich and famous as well as the non-rich and infamous. He ended up writing 37 books - many of them humorous stories about the people he met.

Now, how did H. Allen Smith go from having an 8th grade education to becoming a writer, journalist and best-selling author?

And how did he come up with so many ideas that he could write daily columns, articles, not to mention 37 books?

The answer to the first question is simple: An 8th grade education in the early 1900's was worth its weight in gold compared to the advanced college degree of today. Smith was not alone as a famous writer with far less schooling than "you're supposed to have."

As for question number two on idea generation, this answer is even simpler than the first. As I've been telling y'all all along, everything that happens in your life is material, including what you were dreaming about while sleeping.

Most writers and writing teachers get this wrong. They teach you to remove "Me, myself and I." They tell you to leave yourself completely out of your writing.

Yes, you can do this and become a great writer - but the same is also true when you do the opposite of what the teacher's and copywriting coaches teach.

There's plenty of room for both.

In fact, there's plenty of room for a combination of both, i.e. you can write yourself invisible or you can be partially or completely naked in your verbiage.

I can go on and on about this subject, giving you details upon details, but this is a big chunk of meat to digest today, so I'll end with this consequential fact:

Low Man On a Totem Pole was NOT a known phrase or idiom before H. Allen Smith wrote that book.

Don't know if that's inspiring to you, but it is to me.

Wouldn't it be cool to write a book, and the title or even a word or phrase you used became a household saying that everyone repeats for decades or centuries, yet almost no one knows where it originated, or who coined it?

More meat for thought, eh?

Here endeth the lesson!

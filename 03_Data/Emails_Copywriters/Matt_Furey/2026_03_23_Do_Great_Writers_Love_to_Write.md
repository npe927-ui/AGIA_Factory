Do Great Writers Love to Write?

In 1989, Marsha Sinetar's book, Do What You Love and the Money Will Follow, was released.

'twas a catchy title that contained helpful information. But was it true for those who write? Does money follow because you love to write?

It most certainly could, but there are always exceptions to the so-called rules - and when it comes to writing there are millions of exceptions.

Great writers, including those who have made a lot of moohlah from their writing, don't necessary love writing? Not one bit. In fact, some pretty big names will tell you they "hate it."

So why do they do it?

Sometimes it's because they love the money it makes them, not the other way around.

Another reason I have that some prominent writers have stated goes approximately this way: "I hate writing but I love seeing the result, the finished product."

Yet another question is this: Are there people who love writing who stink well below the low heavens?

You might be surprised to uncover that number, if it were possible to calculate.

No matter how much you hate writing, though, if you have the talent and if you are willing to do "the work," you can make a pile of dough.

On the other hand, if you love writing but you don't do it, your love for the matter doesn't really matter, does it?

On top of that, if you love writing but you prefer to live in a vacuum, learning nothing and letting no one read your words to offer guidance, chances are excellent that the vacuum will do more for you than suck. It will suck you dry.

When I was much younger, I loved wrestling. The sport intrigued me, but as far as talent goes, I was middle-of-the-road. But because I lacked great talent, I studied, practiced and stayed in learning mode. It took a while, but I eventually got good. Some would even say I got really good. I'm not one of those people.

As for writing, in the beginning it wasn't something I  loved or hated. Writing was merely something I wanted to do from time to time. Here and there. Mostly there.

But when my desire to write became an ambition, a "here and now" action backed by a strong and over-arching desire, one that was connected to goals such as writing a book that gets published, having a magazine column,  getting my articles accepted, writing money-making advertisements and info-taining emails, not only did I DO all of the above, but in the doing of them, I improved.

A lot.

A mountain goat climbs vertical mountains and jumps from one cliff to another, regardless of how deep the abyss below it is. It's what it is wired to do. It doesn't think about whether it loves climbing and jumping, nor should a writer.

You write, regardless of whether you love it or hate it. But the truth is, after five minutes of writing, you probably won't know the difference.

Many writers hold themselves back, thinking that quality is the hallmark of greatness.

Oddly enough, the best way to improve your writing is not by focusing on quality. Instead you focus on quantity and this improves the quality more than anything else.

It's not about hard, hard work either. It's mostly about volume and mileage.

The greater the quantity, the greater the chance that the quality of your work will greatly surpass those who focus on quality.

Striving for perfection, then, is not the secret of superior writing.

In my books, I always make sure to leave in one deliberate thai-po. I do this as a reminder that it's not about being perfect. It's about getting to the finish line.

Perfection and the love of writing are not the keys that unlock the door to dollars. Quantity that turns into quality and is mixed with desire are.

If you want to be published and sell a million copies of a book, or if you want more business from ads and emails, the more output you put out there the better you get.

Perfectionistas don't practice. Seeing their mistakes upsets the ego, so they think that not writing will tame their ego. It does nothing of the sort.

The ego is designed to protect you, to keep you away from that which you fear. Ironically, this means that the egotistical writer who puts his or her work forward to be analyzed, judged and perhaps criticized, is far less egotistical than the perfectionist who won't.

Sometimes I hate writing. Sometimes I enjoy it.

But do I ever looooove writing?

The answer to that question is that I've never really thought about it. 'twould be the same as asking an infant who is scribbling with crayons if he loves himself. Huh?

Do the thing and you'll acquire the skill and the power, whether you love doing it or not.

And when you get to the finish, that's when you can talk about love.

Here endeth the lesson.

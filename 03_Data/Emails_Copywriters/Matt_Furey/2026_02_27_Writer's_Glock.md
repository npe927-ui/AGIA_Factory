Writer's Glock

I enjoy pretending that I have no idea what to write about, especially when I don't have anything worth writing about, which is always and never.

There's never anything worth writing about and yet there always is.

The difference is YOU.

Are you willing to put something in writing, even when that something appears to have no merit because it's all jumbled up inside your head?

This morning I imagined that a friend texted me, and come to think of it I might actually be remembering that he sent me a text. It matters not. Neither way.

Everyday I sit behind my fingers with a somewhat worthy goal of writing something stupid, something foolish, something that makes no sense, only to see if the nonsense will turn into common cents.

This goal is not lofty. It is not pie in the sky. But boy, or girl, or somewheres in between, it always comes with plenty of surprises. It's similar to playing peekaboo with your "self" in the mirror.

Is that image you're seeing in the mirror really your "self?" And how would you know, for sure?

The same is true with writing. When you let yourself go, sometime later you might be surprised how often you can look back at something you wrote moons ago and say to your "self," "Who the hell wrote this? Did I really write this?"

And it's not because what you wrote was so bad, but it could be.

Case in point was a client, Buzz, whom I spoke with two nights ago. He's been cranking as a writer for eight years now, and he told me that when he looks at what he wrote back in the early days he doesn't even recognize who wrote it. Yes, it was that bad, according to him - but the people who received his services back then loved it - and I guess that matters, too.

I've heard it said that the secret of getting yourself to write is to imagine a gun to your head, and if you don't get it done, you're done.

I prefer to imagine a glock being the cure for writer's block, even though I don't own one, and never will unless I need to shoot myself in the foot to keep myself out of the bars, which I never go to anyway.

And speaking of bars, strong drink, or liquor is also something that many writers have used sense time became a thing to track, to lubricate their fingers and relax their elbow joints, and speaking of joints, some writers start their day with chicken pot pie, and they use more pot than chicken in their pie.

As for myself, I'm the ultimate bore. No liquor. Definitely no beer. And no MaryJane.

I inhale and exhale through my nose as I practice all these silly putty martial arts moves, many of which are me fighting against my own shadow, or the man in the mirror. It's tarded without the "re" - but yet it works for me.

The moral of this story is that there isn't one. There's no point to be made. There's no advice, wisdom or action steps.

But then again, if you read something in between the lines of what was written, if you find the common cents within the nonsense, then you might just end up being berry, berry rich.

Here endeth the non-lesson.

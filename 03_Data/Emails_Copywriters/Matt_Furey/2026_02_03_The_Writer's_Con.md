The Writer's Con

Matt,
I've heard that you don't believe that writing is hard work. Come on, man! I busted my butt learning how to write. Believe me, learning to write is hard, hard work. So what gives with this con game you're playing, telling people that writing is easy? Explain yourself, please!

Michael Angleless

Michael,
It's all a matter of perspective. Suppose a group of us could play Peeping Tom. And we could peep through the window to watch Stephen King writing The Dead Zone, or Sylvester Stallone writing Rocky.

What would you see?

Would you see them sweating blood, as so many writers proclaim? Would you see them grunting and groaning as they write by hand or let their fingertips do the talking? Would you see them stopping to apply an ice pack on their elbow tendonitis while nursing a torn rotator cuff?

I don't think so.

Hard, hard work is what ditch diggers, stone masons and carpenters do.

Writers who insist they are working hard have never worked a physically demanding job in their lives, otherwise they would be laughing about their jobs while reciting the mantra, "A pencil is lighter than a shovel."

And thank God it is.

Now, there are times when you're not writing, when you are hamstrung for ideas. You may consider this hard work - but yet the reality is, if you're not writing you're not "working." Your brain is engaged but you aren't.

During this non-working time, your subconscious mind is also working, it's drumming up some great material for you from the immaterial - but YOU - you still haven't done a doggone thing other than ask yourself some questions such as, "What's a good title for my book?"

The truth is that most great writing is being done for the writer as he or she does other things, such as sleeping, walking or showering.

Sure, at some point you sit your rumpus in a chair (unless you have a standing desk), and you write - but that's NOT the hardest part. The hardest part was the work that was being done for you subconsciously, when you were resting.

Writers are not blue collar or white collar workers, unless they're priests and/or insist on wearing a suit and tie.

Writers are "no collar players." They wear no collar as they play with words.

You can call "word play" work, or even hard, hard work if you so choose - but if you really stand back and observe the observable, writing is far from it.

If you want to write more, write better and write faster - take some deep breaths and relax. Go for walks, take naps, take a sauna, take plenty of showers and get lots of sleep because that's where the hulk and hallmark of writing are done.

The time you spend writing may feel difficult and puzzling at times - but compared to cutting down trees, laying asphalt and mining for coal, it sure ain't?

# What to Write When You Don't Know Nuthin

**Fecha:** Thu, 19 Mar 2026 14:09:38 +0000
**ID:** 19d066e38296e376

I don't know Dick, much less Harry, when it comes to drawing, and so what did I do? I wrote about them, (or should I say IT?) so that i could learn about it.

When you write about something you are learning, even from the almost beginner stage, you accomplish two things: You learn about the subject and you learn to write.

So many people got it bassackwards. They think they have to already be an expert to write about something. But this ignores the fact that great ideas often come from "inperts," those on the outside looking in. Those who see the whirld through the eyes of a beginner.

Seeing life through the eyes and ears of a beginner is something the Zen Masters idealized.

And one of the treasured hallmarks of a Zen writer is deliberately leaving a mistake in what you have written, so that you realize that perfection is a misguided, somewhat bone-headed goal.

When writing a book, I go through multiple edits. I do my best to make the verbiage as clean as possible, but at some point you need to make the call and say, "Enough. It's good enough. Publish it NOW - or perish."

After you publish what you've written, there's a chance that it will outlive you. It could live closer to the edge of immortality than you ever will. And that's a cool idea, if you ask me.

Let your writing stand as a monument of cherished moments. A reflection of all the times you faced a blank sheet of paper, the times when you didn't know nuthin, yet you still made some uncommon cents from all the  nonsense.

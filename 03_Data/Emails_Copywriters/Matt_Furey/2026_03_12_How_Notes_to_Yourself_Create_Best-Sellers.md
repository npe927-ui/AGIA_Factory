# How "Notes to Yourself" Create Best-Sellers

Over 30 years ago, when I was living in Santa Cruz, California, I came across a small book that had already sold a million copies.

The book, Notes to Myself, by Hugh Prather, eventually sold over five million copies.

How many words were in the book?

I didn't count but if I were to guess, I'd say less than 10,000, and possibly closer to 5,000.

At the time I was flabbergasted. How could a book that was so sparse with words sell so many copies?

Today I know why and how this can happen way better. Way, way better.

Historically speaking, selling millions of copies of a short book is nothing new. Around 2,500 years ago, the Tao Te Ching, a 5,000 word treatise/book, was written by Lao Tzu. It's still selling today - and has been translated into more than 250 languages.

Then again, there's The Cat in the Hat, written by Dr. Seuss, in 1957, with an estimated 10 million copies sold. In my estimation, that estimate has to be off by millions.

So strike from your mind the idea that a book is 200+ pages long with at least 70,000 words.

A book is whatever you decide to make it. You choose the length, the number of words, the title and everything else.

But the "choosing," that's the "problem" for so many people even though it isn't.

I say it's a riddle, not a problem. So instead of beating my brains out to solve "the problem," I give myself space to solve the riddle.

Last time I checked, people love riddles. But problems? Don't know about you but I hate problems.

As a riddle-solveing writer, it's good to know that you can take the high word count road - or you can take the low word count road and earn a fortune.

Either way, everything starts with a pen in hand; a pen you use to write all the ideas that come to mind; the stupid, the silly, the nonsensical and the so-called dumb ones that make cents. Lots and lots of cents.

Make sense?

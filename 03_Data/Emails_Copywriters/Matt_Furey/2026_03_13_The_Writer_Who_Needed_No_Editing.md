# The Writer Who Needed No Editing

**Fecha:** Fri, 13 Mar 2026 16:35:12 +0000
**ID:** 19ce80d54f282621

Back in 1989, I was riding the pine at a self-development seminar in San Jose, California, listening to a speaker whom you've probably heard of, casting what appeared to be a bunch of pearls before us, also known as "the swine."

The speaker said, "Ralph Waldo Emerson's essays were written without correction."

This one phrase planted an "I should" ideal in my mind that took me several years to cough up.

I coughed that ideal up one afternoon, six years later, when I was speaking with Sandy Lydon, a client whom I was training in my gym in Soquel. Sandy was a professor in Japanese and Chinese history at Cabrillo College and was already a published author.

In my extreme naiveté, I asked him, "Why not just leave your writing alone, unedited, the way artists and sculptors do?"

Sandy raised his eyebrows and held them aloft for a few pregnant seconds, switched his lips back and forth, then said, "Well, uh, words are... words are much more malleable than art."

Although Sandy got part of his answer wrong, I didn't know at the time that artists also edit their work. Hire a cartoonist and you'll quickly find this out.

In that same exercise session, another dumb question arose from my prepubescent publishing mind. I asked Sandy, "But what about well-established best-selling writers such as Stephen King, Pat Conroy, James Michener? Surely they don't need any editing?"

I had no idea that my use of the word "surely" in the foregoing sentence signified unbridled arrogance, much the same as someone beginning a sentence, especially in writing, with the word "obviously."

"Obviously, you know that..."

Surely, I don't give a damn for the word "obviously."

Anyway, to answer my buffoonish question, Sandy began with "HA!" and followed this guttural exclamation with, "THEY get edited more than anyone."

Those words caused me to recall what I heard in that self-development seminar in San Jose, about Ralph Waldo Emerson, and how he supposedly wrote all his essays without a single correction.

True?

Not in a million, quadrillion or octillion years.

Great writing is produced from great editing.

In her book, Spiritual Writing, Deborah Herman wrote about people who send their manuscripts to her husband, Jeff, with a mandate from heaven that reads as follows:

"This book was channeled from a Higher Power. Nothing is to be edited."

"Be that as it may," Deborah will say, "Your book needs some editing."

All of this is encouraging.

I take offense to what I'm about to write, but it's good to know that even the Word of God got (and still gets) edited. Knowing this puts all of us and the words we tinker with in a brighter light.

Editing shines a light on what you wrote so that it can shine brighter in the minds of those who read what you have written.

Yes, I have written some pieces and sent them out without editing a single word. That's email. But it is NOT a good idea when you have a book you want to make a fortune with.

You can write the first draft of your book in a short period of time. It doesn't take long if you focus.

Sylvester Stallone wrote Rocky in three and a half days, but the editing of that draft, that's what got it on the big screen and won him an Oscar.

As I wrote yesterday, a book can be short, 5,000 to 10,000 words. With that many words, the editing doesn't take long and the rewards can be HUGE.

You can crank out the first draft for a book of that length in seven days or less, and without even trying.

Each month I produce two newsletters that are 6,000-10,000 words. They don't take long to write and I do my own editing for them. I also get paid handsomely for these newsletters, but I'll stop there lest you think I'm bragging too much on myself.

The purpose of these newsletters is to give readers the innermost scoop within the overall scoop of what they've already learned from me and be rewarded in a big way for doing so.

This is something to keep in mind when you get your book published. You don't just write a book and that's the end of the road. No. The road continues with a monthly newsletter where you reinforce and connect the bridge you've already crossed, so readers can cross that same bridge, too.

Yes, you can write a book and publish it without editing, and forego a monthly newsletter, but if you want to have impact, and you want to be richly rewarded, you follow the process that is proven.

One last takeaway, Emerson could write as well as edit. And if he were alive today and I got a hold of him, his essays would not just be confined to his books; they would also be his monthly newsletters.

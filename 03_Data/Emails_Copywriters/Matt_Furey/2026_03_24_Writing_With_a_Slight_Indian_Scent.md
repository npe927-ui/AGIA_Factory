Writing With a Slight Indian Scent

Husbands, wives, children and neighbors - what do they all have in common?

They supply material for the writer who isn't a narsisimp - whatever that is.

Being my wife is of Chinese descent, I love how she gets some words wrong, but in the right way. One afternoon as we were driving along, she began telling someone who was having trouble making her mortgage payment.

"She's really worried that her house is going to go into forced closure," she said.

"Forced closure?" I repeated.

"Yes, forced closure. Did I say that right?"

"It's called foreclosure" I said.

"Oh, is that how you say it? I always thought it was forced closure."

"Well, come to think of it, it really ought to be called forced closure because that's what it is. You're forced out of your home."

And this leads me to a story of a marketeer I know who used to write great copy, in English, and did so with a slight Indian accent.

I loved reading his copy. It pulled me in. It hooked me.

Everything he wrote had an unusual, unique and spicy flair. I couldn't help but read the words he wrote because they were authentic Indian quizzine. His words gave me pause, made me stop, think and wonder.

Then one day he went on a date with A.I., fell in love and the magnifa scent, the aroma he once wrote with is gone. No more herbage in his verbiage. No more spice. Everything is too nice.

He sends me emails. I read the first sentence and I can "smell that smell." It's no longer a good smell.

Sometimes I push myself to read the second sentence - and if I really want to get pissed on, the third and fourth. I shake my head in disbelief. The magic is gone. It's been replaced by a dull, boring neutered program.

It's a shame. A doggone shame.

I don't have the heart to tell him. He won't believe me if I did.

Even so, this story illustrates why you needn't fear being replaced by A.I.

The more people replace their own authentic accents and voices with fake food, the greater the hunger for a real meal.

Here endeth the lesson.

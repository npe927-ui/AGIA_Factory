Question #3

How do you draw the fine line between being authoritative with clients and showing them that you are in charge without pissing them off?

______________________________________________________________________

You pay attention to how you succeed with azzholes and how you fail with situations you thought you had covered. 

Another process! Life’s full of them, it turns out. 

You take what works and start fussing with it. You unburden yourself of what doesn’t work. 

(And you focus on the stuff that wins over some in a crowd, but turns off the majority. That’s sometimes a recipe for huge success, if who you’re winning over are the rich bastards.)

And sometimes, the people you piss off become your best customers. 

Because not much changes from what worked back in third grade, where the classmate you threw punches with quickly becomes your ride-or-die partner for life.

Question #4

What’s the best way to get an advanced level copywriter to mentor me if I'm low on both experience and money?

______________________________________________________________________

This is a biggie. And I have lots to say here. I’ve even written about this in my books, blog posts and courses (especially The Freelance Manual https://m190.isrefer.com/go/flcwc/jcemail/).  

There’s no simple answer, because writers worthy of being mentors are few and far between.

(I myself am done with it. I mentored nearly a dozen writers over the years, and enjoyed it. A few are still very good friends of mine. I’m just on a different path these days.) 

Even if there was a nice big mob of them, the line waiting for spots would be around the block. 

Because getting personal help from a professional who’s already busted the code on getting ahead in a niche gig is — in my experience -- like climbing aboard a rocket that roars into the stratosphere the second you sit down. It’s life-changing.

Still, there are ways to increase your odds of finding a mentor.

I was infamously lucky. My first "spend a lot of time together working" mentor was Jim Rutz, the guy who not only perfected several methods of getting paid twice what clients used to pay…

…  but also getting paid royalties that nearly caused some of the honchos at the big newsletter publishing joints to faint.

During this same time period, I met Jay Abraham, and through Jay, I met Gary Halbert. In each of these relationships, the term "mentor" was used a lot.

There were no contracts. 

Just handshakes and a brisk "Get to work, lackey" as I was essentially pushed into the deep end of the pool to sink or swim. 

This kind of mentoring inhales your entire life. It’s not a casual thing. The mentor asks for something, you dump everything else on your plate and get to work. 

So, do you still crave mentorship with a top writer? 

Are you ready to uproot your entire life, and move somewhere else for a couple of intense years that leave almost zero room for a decent social life?

If you’ve gotten to the point, professionally, where you are confident in your ability to write fundamentally sound sales pitches in multiple formats, for all kinds of different markets, and meet deadlines like your life depended on it…

… while being absolutely okay with busting your hump like an indentured servant and not seeing much of the windfalls you help create…

… then you might be a good candidate for personal mentoring. 

A good first step, I might suggest, is to put it out there in the universe that you really are open to the rigors of real mentorship and would welcome the opportunity. 

You do this by putting yourself into positions where you meet and shake hands, and you offer to do grunt work for them, for free, to prove your worth. 

* Research. 

* Drafts of bullets. 

* Hundreds of headlines. 

* Tracking down happy customers and sorting out their testimonials. 

Anything that needs a human to pull off.

(I’m purposely not addressing AI here, because whatever advice I offer will morph and mutate within the next month. I’m sure you’ve already learned a bit on how to use AI as a tool for writing. Some clients are trying to get away with having a robot write emails and such, but human empathy, savvy, and salesmanship remains elusive for the clunkers.)

Mentorship is a full-time gig, akin to going to graduate school for a big damn degree. (Though it will always be worth much, much more.)

But there are multiple other ways to glean what you need from the masters around you, and you can control the means the entire time.

So here’s an "out" for you, if you’re not ready to upend your life just to get some life advice: 

Start by observing and listening to what successful people say and do. 

My first early-career "mentors" were all first-rate businessmen, writers, and goal achievers.

And they never suspected they were teaching me anything. 

Let us know how it goes for you!

Question #5

How have people changed over the course of your career?  How have we stayed the same? Of course I’m asking from a selling perspective, but wildly curious on your other observations as well.

______________________________________________________________________

The rapid changing of technology has vividly affected the way people operate in the real world, I’ll tell you that. 

Everything from dating, to surviving the workplace soap operas, to social interaction and expectations of privacy…

… are utterly unrecognizable to all older generations. 

Everything, it seems, has changed or vanished or mutated into an unrelenting sci-fi novel…

… while, astoundingly, not actually changing the basic human experience very much at all. 

The vast majority of folks out there are lonely, bored, frustrated, and suffering from traumatic levels of unhappiness. 

The sociopaths continue to thrive, and even seem to feel more emboldened to take advantage of the ever-growing populations of folks who remain gullible, no matter how often they are cheated. 

And, as ever throughout history, the fundamentally good guys are forever in the deep minority.

Introspection, critical thinking, empathy and doing the right thing without expectation of reward are still trampled and abused by the greater numbers of folks with starvation mindsets ("anything you achieve takes away from whatever I can achieve"). 

And the stupidity. 

Oh my god, the aggressive stupidity out there…

… geez louise, it’s an epidemic now. Or perhaps it’s been like this always, and we’re just seeing more humiliating, gruesome and slanderous videos on social media. 

So it seems like it’s increased, when it’s just being documented more often. 

I do know that classic salesmanship still does the job. 

People are still openly cynical about being "sold", but they don’t understand much about it. 

So good salesmen just pivot to "persuading stubborn cynics" mode, as they have since the dawn of time, and make the sale anyway. 

I started paying close attention to the details of people’s lives back when I was a kid. I had all the freedom a budding juvenile delinquent could desire — I just had to be home for dinner. (I ditched being a JD (juvenile delinquent) as a career choice at twelve, happily.)

And I was baffled that so many other kids I came across seemed almost like prisoners in their own homes. 

Couldn’t run off anywhere without the parent knowing everything about the adventure — who else was going, exactly where and for how long they’d be gone…

… and was that instigator Carlton gonna be around to get them in trouble? 

I was fascinated and horrified at the restrictions so many kids lived under. As well as the dangers of not having at least a modicum of adult supervision for the important matters (like not ditching school). And the cruelties some kids suffered — almost always in silence, cuz nobody listened — still haunt me. 

The real shock, though, was growing up and STILL finding out that most people lived cramped lives void of adventure. 

The fears of social interaction or confrontation still rob so many of a happy existence. 

Not fitting in creates frustration and anger and impotent rage, despite the promises that adulthood would bring us all agency and self-determination. 

I’ve been telling people for decades now that we’re long overdue for a new punk movement. 

Not just in music, but in the way we approach life itself. 

We’re here for such a short ride, it seems like shaking things up should be a desirable goal, not something to crush and eliminate a-borning. 

I think I’ve seen bits of healthy rebellion happening around the edges of the culture, and I welcome it. We seem to be slipping back way too easily into censorship and exile and outright punishment for non-conformance. 

And that’s been bullshit since I first heard of Maynard G. Krebbs. (Boomer reference to an ancient TV show. Carry on)

That’s why I’ve always tried to shake people up at my events. 

Get them off their "usual" behavior, and experience some safe breakthroughs. (I used to demand that people at seminars go have lunch with strangers each day. No hanging out only with the spouse or biz partner or familiar folks. And they grumbled, but did it… and many discovered amazing new friendships that last to this day. And it was such a mild little rebellious act…)

We’re still shaved apes with one foot in the jungle and one sizzling on the hot asphalt of civilization. 

We weren’t built for modern life, but here we are… and some of us have adapted better than others. 

I’ve heard historians say that if we shared the same language, we’d get along just as well with an ancient tribe of Neanderthals as we would today with the other parents at a little league game. 

Meaning, humans haven’t changed much at all, fundamentally. 

It’s only the sense of modernity that makes us feel special and different.

Question #6

Dude! This stuff is so good!

How about sharing the list of 150 Boomer songs? This Boomer would love to have more songs that I could show off my 50+ years of 4 chords mastery to family and friends! 

______________________________________________________________________

Thanks for the compliments. We do our best over here, trying to provide value in a world forever seeking to make a buck. 

But, no. Can’t share that list of songs, cuz it was all written down on bar napkins and scraps of paper that are lost to history. I occasionally sit down and make new lists, but that’s not simple work.

Here’s another challenge for you: 

Look up the Billboard charts for the years you were still a ravishing young animal bursting with vigor and verve…

… and write down all the songs that still give your heart a little pop. 

Then look up the music for each tune. There are apps for getting tabulature for guitar (if you don’ t know how to read music), and still a few sites available that offer the chords and lyrics to songs for free.

* If you play solo, make a list of the songs you can play with whatever talent or skill you already have. Chuck the complicated stuff. 

* If you play with other people, work through some material, see what lights everybody’s fire. 

Eventually, you’ll come up with a set list that gets listeners singing along, or patrons dancing (or crying their beer), and you’ll encounter requests, and other lists of songs others have worked out already. 

Always great to hear from another musician! Let me know how it goes. 

Stay frosty,

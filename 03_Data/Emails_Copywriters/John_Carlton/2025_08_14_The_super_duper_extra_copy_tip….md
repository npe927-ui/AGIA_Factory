People always think I’m lying about this…Howdy.

People often ask me for “extra” secrets to getting really good at marketing and copywriting. 

I mean, I’ve been teaching this stuff for years… 

… but what they really want is the juicy, super duper secret tactic I’ve been keeping from everyone else.

So here you go:

Here’s one of my extra-advanced secrets:

Become a student of history. 

Now you know why I don’t share the secret too often. 

It’s not glamorous and it reeks like work. 

But it’s incredibly effective, and all the more so because so few people want to dive into the past. 

Not just of advertising history, but also:

*  our culture

* language 

* art and war 

* technology

When we take things for granted, we’re thinking inside the box and stunting our imagination.

History is the easiest way to expand your consciousness and to get the Big View of life (where all the truly mind-blowing revelations like to gestate).

So learn about the golden age of the super-agency, when John Caples was still around, Rosser Reeves was just getting revved up, and David Ogilvy was writing his most famous copy. 

Most of the ad and copywriting books on my shelf are from this period.

Sure, the ads are all about slogans, with lots of graphics…

… but back then, the salesmen were still in charge.

It was a different world…

…and you gotta wonder how anything ever got done when nearly every guy in the agency started drinking — heavily — at noon every day. 

(In fact, you were regarded with suspicion if you weren’t a lush. (No promotion for you, Mr. Teetotaler.))

You might laugh at how naive they seemed back then.

But you can draw a straight line from the online advertising of today, clean through those late-fifties/early-sixties days, and back to the “official” beginnings of direct response in the heydays of the late 1800s.

Expand your horizons. 

Start by catching a couple of “Mad Men” reruns. 

If anyone asks, you can call it homework.

Stay frosty,

John

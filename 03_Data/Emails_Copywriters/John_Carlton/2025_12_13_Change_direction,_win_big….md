Howdy.

You'd be surprised at how many people land exalted, high paying positions primarily by presenting themselves as "decisive". 

They could be totally unqualified otherwise. 

Being decisive doesn't always mean good decisions, mind you.

And a lot of people seem to think standing by a bad decision carries some
kind of moral weight.

In business, sticking to a bad decision under a delusion that you are a "man of principle" will bankrupt you.

When at a fork in the road, first get your hands on as much data and input as you can. 

Start by defining what a "big decision" even means.

Choosing the wrong sandwich or coffee flavor has no disastrous consequences.

So be quick and decisive and move on. 

On the other hand, if you're considering...

* Buying a house
* Getting married
* Robbing a bank... etc ...

...  these are situations so loaded with consequences, you'd have to be an idiot to do them on a hunch.

Mind you, the mortgage offices, divorce courts, and jails are crammed with just such idiots.

So don't use them as your role models here.

We're in the Information Age. 

You can't open your device without tripping over data and info. 

Without moving a muscle, you can search pretty much the entire library of human knowledge for almost anything you want to know.

You have no
excuse for relying on hunches for the big decisions.

However... there are smaller - yet still critical - decisions to be made each day that you can't research or get data on.

You really do have to trust your gut on some things.

Like deciding, hours before a deadline, to trash a sales pitch that you've finally decided isn't working... and rewrite the entire thing. 

As a professional, you go beyond the tidy boundaries of what works for everyone else. 

You have to meet the deadline, yes... 

... but up until that last ticking second, you are focused like a bulldog after a rabbit on the bottom line: 

The success of your campaign.

It's damned inconvenient for the process to work like this. 

But sometimes, it just does.

So adopt the attitude of the most successful business owners - total flexibility, and a happy embrace of cold, hard reality. 

It's like driving through a green light.

Sure, you're in the right, but if you see some drunk bozo careening through the red
light, you stop anyway.

The ability to make a good decision is a key to becoming a true professional, in any market or business. 

One of your best first decisions? 

Learn how to sell already. 

And if you've got the basics, get better.

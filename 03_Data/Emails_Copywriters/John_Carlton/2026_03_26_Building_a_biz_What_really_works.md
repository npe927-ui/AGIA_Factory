As a copywriter and marketing consultant for going on 4 decades now, I’ve received a lot of questions over the years.

Mostly, they revolve around practical topics...

... like how to: 

* sell more stuff 

* increase conversions

* come up with eye-grabbing headlines

* craft irresistible bullets

* create compelling offers that are such a scorching hot good deal that a prospect would have to be insane to say "no" 

And so on. I love it, because that’s what I live for.

But if you haven’t even gotten that far, you might be flummoxed by the most basic question of all:

How do you launch a company and become an entrepreneur in the first place?

Instead of yapping on about what you need; tenacity, perseverance, gonads the size of Utah, etc…

… I thought it might be equally as valuable to talk about what you DON’T need. 

There’s been a ton of confusion over that matter since the dawn of time. 

With the Internet and the explosion of WYSIWYG creation software for video, software and apps, it gets even more bewildering. 

(For the dinosaurs among us, I sympathize. WYSIWYG stands for "what you see is what you get." It means you can do tech stuff without having to learn the code. You’re welcome.)

So to help you avoid chasing the wind and wasting a whack of time on exhausting activity that does nothing to actually bring in the moolah…

… here is a short list of what you DON’T need to get going. 

(Some of these may or may not come in handy later, but start simple so you actually get a success or two under your belt.)

* Gobs of start-up capital

* A stunning logo 

* Flashy whiz-bang tech

* Amazing graphics or a cool logo

* A biz or marketing degree from Harvard

* A fancy workspace (My first "office" rotated between my car and a buddy’s couch after I lost my job, my girlfriend and my apartment, all within a month of each other. Seriously.)

* Fame or genius levels of talent

You also don't need to know everything from day one or have narcissistic levels of confidence to get going. 

When I got started, I was a clueless slacker with zero confidence that I could "do this." 

After an epiphany that no one was coming to rescue me from my own mess, I made a commitment to go all in with this copywriting thing. 

And I second guessed myself every step of the way. 

But eventually, I managed to reach the high, upper echelons in the marketing world… 

… because I kept things simple and focused on what really mattered:

A workable idea, a hungry market...

... and the ability to structure a business with a solid foundation that will withstand every disaster and disruptive tech the world throws at it.

Question 1: 

On your storied rock & roll sabbatical, what were your top 5 favorite songs to play? 

______________________________________________________________________

I’m actually flattered that so many folks are still interested in my little sojourn into the world of playing music in seedy biker bars (during my 4th mid-life crisis) (still the best one I’ve had). 

My god, the drama and undiluted joy of hitting a groove with a hot band while drunks dance on the bar and third-rate romances do their Romeo and Juliet moves in the dark corners. 

There’s nothing like it anywhere else. 

Learned a lot of lessons I later put to good use when I started speaking on stages at marketing seminars around the globe. From how to get an audience on your side quickly — sometimes by aggressively insulting them and challenging them to participate instead of sitting there like dead props — to sensing the prevailing mood and using it to make everyone deeply invested in what you’re gonna say next. 

Also: 

How to deal with prima donna musicians (which includes most of the good ones), how to convince skeptical know-it-alls into following your "biz plan" for the gig, and when to haul out the Big Guns to resuscitate a fading crowd.

So much can be learned from a gig lasting into the wee hours. All easily translatable to marketing persuasion tactics and copy. 

I actually found an old playlist, too. I’d put together nearly 150 songs that any Boomer musician with experience would probably know how to play. Simple stuff, with a minimum number of chords or key changes or obscure bridges…

… that would be recognizable as "oooh, that’s a good one" to any crowd who ever listened to a damn good jukebox.  

Keeping it all simple was important, cuz I’d often meet half the band for the first time in the parking lot right before we climbed up on the stage and set up. I would stake out a spot where the bass player and keyboard player (when there was one) could easily see my hands. And I had long ago learned how to lead a band with head bobs and body movements (plus the occasional shout). 

That list was priceless. Even skeptical musicians respected the genius of having such a great array of tunes we could jam on without rehearsal. And word got out amongst the locals, bringing us well-known saxophonists and keyboard wizards who had heard that once we hit a groove, we filled the joint with solos and never, ever expected anyone to do "covers" of the original tune. 

We weren’t a cover band, by any stretch. 

We were passionate, tipsy musicians looking to kick out the jams and get into that precious groove for one night. 

Most professional players seldom get to let loose like that, cuz they’re backing up a singer or an act that likes to copy their albums precisely. 

Booooooring. 

We took risks, flew off the handle and careened off cliffs with glee. 

And the folks lucky enough to be at that bar that night usually sensed they were in for a treat. 

The best drummer I ever played with gave me the best testimonial I've ever had: "Man, you just made every gig fun."

I had only two rules for any song that made the list: It had to either 

* (a) make you wanna cry in your beer (for the rare slow tune we did)…

* or (b) make you wanna shake your booty. 

That kept the ballads and dull stuff completely out of the picture. 

And because we played mostly in Nevada — where there’s no closing time for bars — our sets often went from early evening to nearly dawn. Just a rollin’ and tumblin’ till the cows stumbled home. 

I dearly loved every song I’d worked, over decades, to include on that list. Still, I have some memories of a few that still make me shiver:

1. A medley of Mystic Eyes (written by Van Morrison when he was with the Irish outfit Them, with a relentless Bo Diddley beat) smashing into Rocky Mountain High (a loose interpretation of the James Gang’s hit). One night, somebody spilled beer on my stomp pedals, and they started short-circuiting into gorgeous death-throes, creating sounds I could never find again. Did an entire solo just with barely-controlled reverb feedback, which caused a packed bar to go into a trance while dancing wildly.

Gosh, that was fun. And RIP to those pedals…

2. Mercury Blues, a rollicking 1948 tribute to the Mercury Eight coupe, which we kicked into overdrive. Kudos to Ry Cooder’s inspired cover. 

3. Good Morning, Little Schoolgirl, by Sonny Boy Williamson. We did it through a more driving beat, which kept the band super tight even when newbies were sitting in. (This song was covered by almost every top sixties band, from the Grateful Dead to Ten Years After to the Yardbirds to Rod Stewart to Muddy Waters. And never done the same way twice.)

4. Don’t Bogart That Joint, by the Holy Modal Rounders. A simple riff, but we never saw a barroom crowd not stop what they’re doing and sing along lustily (and often in perfect harmony) when we did this one. Brings a tear to my eye, just remembering how close we felt with all those sodden strangers, late at night while a few possibilities still existed for epic adventures. 

5. Route 66. Classic tune, with multiple opportunities for each musician to go off on a relevant solo. 

That’s enough for now, I think.

Question 2: 

John, I operate in M&A. Here's what I keep running into: business owners who've built something over 20-30 years, know they need an exit plan, tell me all the right things during our conversation and follow-ups.

Then they go GHOST. 

What's your move when someone clearly needs what you're selling, knows they need it, but their identity is so tied to the status quo that saying "yes" feels like losing?

______________________________________________________________________

Ah, marketing & acquisition, I assume. A brutal alternative universe in the biz landscape. 

I completely sympathize with your challenge here. It’s like forcing someone out of the house they’ve lived in and raised families in and tended the back garden with real love. Maybe growing prize roses over the graves of little Muffy the cat and that big goofy dog Bowser. (My Pop planted an orange tree over the spot we buried my Beagle Manfred. Damn tree produced enough oranges every year for the entire neighborhood for decades…)

The "needs" prompting folks to even consider radical changes — such as "exiting" their former life, which consumed and defined them for so long — can seem so superficial and vague. Especially when measured against the emotional payload of memory and history. 

You can’t push people on this. 

You can lay out the most sensible, rational and obviously correct plan possible… and still never get them to agree to it.

Until their emotional needs are dealt with. 

So you gotta reframe everything. It’s not the "end" of anything. It’s a new chapter in the continuing adventure of their lives. 

And they don’t have to like any of it. 

Emotional decisions skirt reason and fact and everything else in the rational case to be made. 

They gotta feel right about it. 

So you have to apply psychology. Usually, the street-level version, too. Greed and fear, often, are good first stops. Feel good about your legacy, and imagine what the incoming money can accomplish. Sure, take a vacation, see the world, fund college for the grandkids. 

But more important, go into your next adventure loaded for bear. Remember how you felt earning that first dollar in your biz when it was brand spanking new? You can, if you want, go after that entrepreneurial rush again. 

Or do something else. 

People who work hard for long periods, loving and sacrificing and bleeding for a biz, are not gonna go quietly even when their better angels are screaming at them to "let it go". 

* Find out what their fears are. 

* Make sure they can feel confident no one will loot their legacy, or disrespect it, or whatever they secretly dread. 

These are humans beings, vulnerable creatures who’ve been operating in reality long enough to know the universe is largely hostile to success and dreams and plans. You probably can’t really guarantee any outcome, if you’re being truthful. 

But you can walk a mile in their shoes, and empathize with what they’re facing. 

Don’t lie or make up shit — if you’re unclear on how they really feel, keep questioning and reframing things until you know. 

Why would you ghost someone coming to you with a solid plan?

Probably because there are outstanding possibilities and outcomes you don’t like thinking about, can’t wrap your head around, or abjectly fear because it feels like admitting defeat. Or, worse, impending death. 

Business stops being about business quickly, when you’re pitching something. 

It’s always personal. 

And it’s always about pain, fear and the mysteries of the future. 

You get to know ‘em, you can bring them home. 

Good luck.

Question 3: 

John, do you believe a non-native speaker can still become a great copywriter?

______________________________________________________________________

Absolutely.

(But with a caveat: your message MUST be clear and easy to understand.)

In fact, I had this exact question put to me in a seminar by a Russian who could butcher an English sentence like nobody’s business. 

For him — who was promoting his own product line — the key was to lean into the fact he wasn’t particularly proficient. He was a proven communicator despite his clipped and choppy elocution…

… and unlike the French (ask me about that waiter near Notre Dame sometime, the arrogant bastard), most Americans are shockingly patient about earnest ESL types. And often love foreign accents and idioms and stories from places they’ve never been. 

Be honest, have a sense of humor about miscommunications, and fine-tune your charm. 

It can work so well, that I’ve known biz owners who speak perfect English prefer to use a heavy accent and throw in a few mistakes just to appear like they’re struggling with translations. 

You’ve got a built-in storyline here. 

Which any savvy sales pro will realize offers amazing opportunities for bonding and persuasion. 

Now, if you’re asking about being a freelance copywriter, working with clients rather than selling your own stuff...

... the answer is a little different. 

You can make a case that your lack of sharp vocabulary or being unfamiliar with slang nevertheless offers you a way to break through with prospects.

Top writers hate the fact that they have to consciously tone down their large vocabulary, and make their copy easily understood at a fifth-grade level.

But if you have the chops to write at that level naturally… 

... you could even beat more veteran writers by being easily understood by entire audiences. 

So, basically, it all depends on you, and your relationship to good salesmanship (where there is always a way around ANY challenge) and your ability to communicate clearly…

… which requires clear thinking and a deep bag of skills and charm. 

All that translates well into any language.

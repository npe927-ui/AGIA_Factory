Yanked from the edge of bankruptcy...…Howdy. 

Here’s your tough reality check for the day.

Was your biz once thriving, bringing in a highly satisfying amount of moolah while being a joy to operate, but now all the excitement is gone?

Over the years, I’ve managed to save big lumbering companies on the verge of bankruptcy.

Often, it came down to something mind-bogglingly simple:

* A single sales letter touting a fire sale (to raise cash)

* Offering a mind-boggling deal loaded with must-have bonuses (to cold-cock the competition)

* Waking up a bored list of past customers and making them fall in love with the client all over again.

Most good business ideas start out simple like this. 

Then, because you’re human and your wiring has some kinks in it, you start adding complexity before you even get off the ground: 

“Hey! We could add partners! And implement 30 new managers! And create a job for my inept brother-in-law…”

This happens so much… on both a micro and macro level.

You gotta train yourself to stop.

Take a step back, and try to see what the shortest path might look like.

For me, all of my ads came down to this: 

* Here’s who I am

* Here’s what I have

* Here’s why you want it

* And here’s what to do now. 

That’s it.

No fluff, no tangents. 

Think of the Big Damn Problem you think is holding you up. 

The answer is almost never to make things MORE complex.

Nope. It’s to simplify. 

Get to the heart of matter, with specifics, and strengthen the fundamentals.

Often, this means beating down what you think you need…

… more elaborate product lines, a call center, a new sales force, maybe a big roll of the dice with an expensive infomercial. And new investors, and a new membership site, and…

STOP. 

Take a deep breath. 

Let it out slowly.

Work with the resources that are already available, so nothing new needs to be created (except maybe a fresh bonus or two). 

By simplifying, you can get your pitch out in front of eyeballs and bringing in results before the bloated schemes of competitors get off the drawing board.

You can always add complexity later on. 

But start by making sure your new idea will be welcome in your niche and your product will fly off the shelves.

* Match your product to the specific needs of the market

* Deliver it efficiently

* Price it to sell 

In other words, offer a great deal, in a great ad, and get it in front of prospects. 

Keep it simple so you don’t get bogged down with endless details and overwhelm.

It’s the best way to get and stay in the game.

never lose sight of these principles…Howdy.

A-List marketers have zero fear of slow sales, a shaky economy, or getting edged out by robots that can do the job in 10 seconds by anyone with a free email account.

Where, oh where do they get this kind of confidence, you ask? 

It all stems from a DEEP understanding of all of the intangibles that go into marketing. 

Much of the craft involves easy-to-understand tactics… 

… like the feature/benefit relationship. (For every feature you write about, attach a benefit.)

But good writers know about the deeper principles that are always at play.

For example:  

No one WANTS to be sold. 

The fine art of salesmanship runs deeper than obvious, hit ‘em over the head with a sledgehammer tactics. 

There’s a subtle psychology at play here. 

Prospects don’t WANT their hopes fired up only to have their hearts and dreams shattered again..

Here’s an example of the progression of simple, feature-laden bullets that move on to address a reader’s deeper worries and insecurities. 

To start with, imagine selling a car. 

Rookie writers think “4,000 horsepower engine” says it all.

A pro will finish the thought by expanding on “And this means…” etc.

So instead of ending at “4,000 horsepower...  

… the pro writer will continue with:

“And this means your new car goes very, very fast, with a deep roar that scares horses and little old ladies, while impressing everyone you need to impress…”) 

(Okay, I’m laying it on thick, but you get the point.)

Features are still really important even though they’re easily understood. 

That’s how you lend credibility to benefits.

But to write at a world-class level, you gotta master the less-easy-to-understand stuff. 

The really cool, “should-be-illegal-it-works-so-good” secrets that only an experienced pro could ever show you.

In other words, the intangibles.

Yes, you can write fairly fast — and even craft a decent pitch — by following templates or ripping existing ads or getting your entire first draft using a chatbot.

But you still need to develop the chops to translate salesmanship into print… 

… taking vague, unconnected ideas and welding them together in a persuasive, ass-kicking pitch that won’t let your prospect sleep until they’ve ordered.

For most of the ads you write, your reader will NOT come along willingly. 

And the reader sure as heck doesn’t TRUST you for a hot second.

Which, for a top writer, is no problem.

With a good hook, they know they can deliver an irresistible pitch.

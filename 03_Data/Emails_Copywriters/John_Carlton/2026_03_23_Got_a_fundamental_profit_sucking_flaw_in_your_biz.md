Here’s the key to fixing it…Howdy.

As a certifiably lazy dude, I’ve always been tempted by shortcuts. 

I mean, why take an hour to drive to the cottage when you could arrive in 45 minutes just by meandering off on a side street to skip the traffic jam? 

If all goes well, you get 15 extra minutes of drinking time and swapping stories. 

It sounds like the smartest idea ever. 

Until you’ve been driving forever in the dark. No road signs anywhere, but it’s 100% certain that at least one axe-murderer is hiding in the ditch waiting for you to get a flat.

And don’t any of you whipper-snappers start yapping about how my smartphone has a GPS. 

You know how I feel about tech. Besides, if you’re going to get lost, you might as well make it count.

But when it comes to shortcuts in marketing, you really want to be careful. 

If I woke up tomorrow and realized the universe had changed in such a way that a decent sales pitch no longer required:

* proof
* credibility
* believability
* and all the other classic ingredients… 

…  I’d be the first one to STOP bothering with long copy.

I don’t write long copy for the joy of it.

I write long copy because that’s what WORKS. 

Still, even in this day and age where everyone has the attention span of a gnat. 

And regardless of the medium.

Mind you, I’d never recommend doing more work than necessary to bring in the moolah. 

But you can’t do LESS than the minimum.

Here’s the process, in a nutshell:

* You start at the beginning of your sales message

* Cover the points your prospects need to hear in order to make a decision

* Urge them toward the right decision (buy your stuff)

* And close with panache.

When you can do that in a few terse sentences, or in a whiz-bang video, let me know. 

But after almost three decades in the front-line trenches of business slogging — through the fog and chaos of multiple technological upheavals — I’m not holding my breath.

Anyway, the nature of business requires flexibility.

And, curiously, this is NOT an age-related thing.

At seminars, I’ve encountered wizened old farts who were cleaning up online, totally hip to every cutting-edge burp and tweak of the Technology Beast… 

.. and I also met bright young would-be-wizards who couldn’t ossify and wall themselves up in a cave fast enough.

And vice versa, of course.

The dudes and dudettes, young and old, who are making it work are flexible jaguars, alert and eager to learn. 

The basic rule is simple and eloquent: 

Things change.

And while that super-tight polyester disco-era leisure suit in the back of your closet may yet come back into vogue… 

… nearly every aspect of successful marketing has moved along at a brisk clip.

The days of easy traffic are as quaint when having a toll-free 800-number was an exotic luxury. 

In other words, it’s ancient history.

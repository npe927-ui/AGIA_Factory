Protect your bottom line from bottom feeders…Howdy. 

Here’s a nasty little lesson in human behavior and how it can eat away at your bottom line.

Sure not the most fun topic, but you have to know how to deal with all kinds if you want to be a true pro…

… even if it means kicking a prospect to the curb every once in a while.

Here’s the sordid story:

A guy emails me a list of questions about my “main” site  (john-carlton.com – if you want to know more). 

The really annoying part?

Only ONE of the questions wasn’t already answered at the site.

Which means the guy didn’t even bother to read the copy before emailing those questions..

So I answered the one question, and referred him back to the site.

He emails back, with more questions. 

Still hasn’t read the copy.

So I punt him over to my long-suffering assistant, who politely tries to answer most of his questions… 

… and then she points him back to the site.

For some reason, this sets the guy off.

He sends a long winded response about his life as a “top businessman” who “doesn’t like surprises” and that’s why he’s asking all these questions. 

My assistant writes back, still polite, explaining how ALL his questions are ALREADY answered. 

At this point, I tell her to stop engaging with the dude. 

He’s not interested in becoming a customer. Ever. 

What this guy wants is his own customized explanation of what I offer - for FREE.

Even if it’s a complete re-telling of everything in the original piece, he needs it recited to him, personally.

Special treatment, in other words. 

Because, you know, he’s a TOP businessman.

This is a form of “Psychic Vampire” behavior. 

This had nothing to do with any lack of clarity on my end, and everything to do with entitlement. 

After, I kid you not, eight emails sent in to us — he finally told us he wasn’t quite ready to buy anything yet, and was gonna think about it.

Needed to kick some more tires, I suppose.

The point of the story is to reiterate the importance of keeping a clean list.

And to avoid spending too much time and effort on looky-loos and pretenders. 

Cuz look, if you’ve read anything I’ve written, then you know I’m not in the habit of leaving out important bits of info when I create copy. 

I over-explain, if anything, because I learned long ago that clarity is the backbone of good marketing.

When you identify these time wasters, my advice is to let them go. 

Sure, I know several marketers who are in love with “crush ‘em with contact” tactics, who swear that some prospects take fifteen or twenty emails or letters to make the buying decision.

In my experience, you’re just asking for trouble. 

Track returns, track problems, and track any name that comes up over and over again in unpleasant ways. 

Some marketers will learn the hard way that sales to certain types of people eventually become a big net loss, chewing up time, energy and money.

It’s like brow-beating someone into marrying you. 

You may succeed, after years of exhausting courtship… 

… only to discover the real game doesn’t start until after the deal is done.

Let the nightmare begin.

I’ll pass, thank you very much.

Stay frosty,

Now where were we?

Ah yes, back to the importance of a clean list. 

I don’t know about you, but I prefer to share my thoughts and wisdom with a few people who know me and are happy to hear what I’ve got to say. 

Keeping things clean and efficient is one of the most underrated things you can do for your business. 

You’ll probably make more money. 

But even if you don’t, you’ll save your sanity.

And there’s definite value there, too. 

No semi-sane person wants a list with numbers all puffed up with egomaniacs. 

Case in point, one dude, who, I kid you not – sent EIGHT emails bombarding my assistant and me with one question after another…

… but the answers were already clearly spelled out on the sales page!

(

**I stand corrected:** there IS such a thing as a stupid question. If the copy you just read clearly states that "X product costs $100" and you respond to ask "how much X costs," I’m gonna write you off as a blithering idiot. But there I go bitching & moaning already.

**Save it for Wednesday, John**.)

There are two potential lessons here:

**One**: 

Make sure you never leave out any important bits when creating your sales copy. 

That way, serious buyers can find what they need to make their decision. Over-explaining is almost always better than leaving gaps.  

**Two:** 

Avoid spending too much time and effort on looky-loos and pretenders. 

When you identify time wasters, let them go. Don’t let the weeds overtake the garden you’ve worked so hard to cultivate. 

At some point, we’ve all learned the hard way that certain types of prospects are a net loss, chewing up time, energy and money.

You may finally get the sale… 

… only to discover the real game doesn’t start until after the deal is done.

I’ll pass, thank you very much.

But maybe that’s just me.

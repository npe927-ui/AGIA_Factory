Question # 1

I would love to help my B2B customers drive sales through referral programs. I would love to get your feedback on what works and what doesn't. 

______________________________________________________________________

I’ve always taught that techniques for B2B and B2C are almost identical… 

… because we’re dealing with humans in both cases. 

Decision-makers in biz don’t stop feeling like frauds, or being jealous about the competition, or losing sleep, terrified of being held accountable for bad moves, just because they have titles. 

Every biz is unique because the people running the joint are different in every case. But when you strip away all the corporate-speak and mission statements and PR… 

… you’re just dealing with flawed humans who respond to the same motives regular folks do. So:

As with any audience: 
Find out what the biggest fears and greed points are with your target audience. 

For biz to biz specifically:
Always ONLY with the dudes and dudettes who make decisions. 

That last statement is the main difference between B2B and B2C. Trying to embolden mid-level managers to take your pitch to the C Suite is a recipe for failure (cuz they’ll never do it right). 

Sounds simple, but it takes a deft touch to even reach the corporate beasts who actually run things. Spend the time to find out who signs the checks, and then do whatever machinations are required to get personalized communication into their hands. 

Most still get FedEx packages, which can often bypass the receptionists who open all their other mail (and will toss your carefully crafted pitches into the round file with the other junk). 

And personal calls, if you can get them, beat out even the most intimate emails you can create, every time. 

An old tactic we used was to deliver a letter via FedEx with a big bill attached (depending on the value of the prospect, it could be a hundred-dollar bill)… 

… and some very personalized copy highlighting the most outrageous benefits we could summon (including saving their job through our offer, during economic upheavals) (which happen often).

Then we’d tell them we’ll call their office at 2pm tomorrow to discuss. And to alert their staff to put the call through. 

Just basic old school salesmanship: 

* Personalized attention

* Empathetic understanding of the "quiet fears" that your stuff solves

* Taking on the risks of expensive contact measures when the sale is big

* Knowing how to reach and talk directly to decision-makers

Hope that helps.

Question # 2

What are your thoughts on branding advertising? More accurately, do you think that the millions of dollars spent by many companies on TV spots and YouTube Ads are justified?

______________________________________________________________________

I’ve always said that if you have a billion dollars and fifty years or so to brand your stuff, go for it. 

The actual secret to blowing away the competition in goods and services is placement, however. I remember watching a Pepsi supplier take all the Coke product from a supermarket’s eye-level shelf and put it on the bottom, and replace it with his bottles. 

That was where the real corporate warfare was being waged. 

At the other end of the marketing spectrum...

... it’s INSANE how many strip-mall businesses attempting to out-clever the competition use cute names that forget to mention the fundamental service and spend their entire budget on design no one cares about. 

(Hair stylists are some of the worst, too. Names like "Scissor Fantasies" or "Locks and Twinkles", while they’re sandwiched in between a hardware store and a bakery.)

And public service announcement: 
If you are a bakery, put the word "bakery" in your damn name! 

You don’t see tax accountants or lawyers getting cute with their… well, wait. I’m now seeing even those fools going for clever over functional. 

I still remember all those pre-tech-crash companies blowing their entire marketing budget on Super Bowl commercials a couple of decades ago. They all went belly-up by year’s end, just like their competitors who didn’t advertise much at all. 

Old school salesmanship is, fundamentally, sensible and realistic. Even if it’s not glamorous or exciting.  

Use the "ask your prospects and customers" tactic of deciding on titles and names, and make sure your emails and ads are the best thing your target audience reads today. 

And use long, super-effective copy and riveting story-driven pitches with enough attitude and outrage to get attention, hold it, and make the sale. 

Always remember that good marketing is a numbers game. You’re only going after a tiny percentage of any target audience, and you want to be able to scale that afterward. 

Money goes out to get attention and bring in hot prospects, and money comes in through sales. That’s the essence of direct response marketing.

And know that, often enough, companies with lousy optics, flawed marketing, and no real attempts at branding… do very, very well.

Because they use salesmanship.

Question # 3

What drove you to go out and do band gigs in one of your "middle-life crises" as you call them?

Boredom, unfulfillment, heartbreak? Just curious.

______________________________________________________________________

For those in the back - or who are new to the list: 

Mid-sixties rock and roll. That’s what got me hooked. 

I formed my first band in junior high school. Played a lot through high school and college… and then, when it became obvious that my best prospects lay outside the music industry, I concentrated on writing and business. 

Which was also a blast…

… but not quite what the doctor ordered.

By the time I turned 40, I was already regularly taking off 3-6 months every year to go roam the world, golf, scuba dive, and indulge in civilization’s best distractions. 

I moved from the beach in LA up to Lake Tahoe during one such interlude, started writing fiction and sitting in with local bands. My hands were showing early signs of arthritis, and I realized my window for one last fling in a real band of my own was vanishing. 

So I lived off royalties, started haunting local open mic nights, found an awesome drummer and an excellent bassist... and spent most of one year playing all the biker bars in Northern Nevada. 

It wasn’t about the money — I gave the drummer my share of the haul from the door. 

It was about that rare feeling you get when the band hits a groove hard, and the joint is bouncing from all the dancing, and everyone enters a trance that ONLY real rock and roll can induce. 

So. Much. Fun. 

I also had experienced a 20-year career as a freelancer at that point, and I knew that bold moves and taking huge risks were how you got what you wanted. 

After a while, it was time to return to biz with my whole heart. And I became the lovable guru you’re dealing with now...

Question # 5

I've been a police officer in Spain for more than 10 years. How would you leverage that in copywriting and sales?

______________________________________________________________________

Well how about that. A "how do you write copy" question," but a very specific one you knew I’d find irresistible. You sneaky bugger. 

(Note: for those new to these Wednesday "Bitch & Moan Mailbox" sessions: One of the rules is no questions that take an entire course to answer. Dude here is breaking the rules, but very clever about it with the whole policeman angle. I still can’t teach you how to write an ad in a single paragraph, but I sure can tell you how it relates to police work.) 

Back in the nineties, there was a book called "What Cops Know."

It detailed, from a cop’s perspective, the behavior of both the guilty and innocent that the author encountered over a long career. 

And it was all incredibly fascinating to us as writers. (Gary Halbert introduced me to it.) 

But not because it was a cop talking about crooks, thieves, ne’er-do-well’s and "I didn’t do it" unlucky bastards. 

No, we loved it because of the psychological insights the book delivered. 

And that’s the main job of any salesman:

To understand, at a level deeper than the busiest shrink, what’s behind the shyte humans do to themselves and others. 

* What motivates them

* What discourages them

* Who they think is behind the grand conspiracy to deny them wealth and happiness. 

During our first Psych Insights For Modern Marketers podcast a few years back, Kevin Rogers and I interviewed his uncle about his career as a cop in Massachusetts. All the weird stuff no civilian could believe actually happened, and all the insane behavior of people facing trauma and disaster. 

It was one of the most popular episodes for our advertising professional audience. 

Make of that what you will. Use what you know, when it’s appropriate, to the right target markets.

Question # 6 

In the age of AI, if I dropped you, John Carlton, veteran marketer in a strange city, with no network and with only $50 to your name, the knowledge in your head, and access to the public library, what would you do to get on your feet?

______________________________________________________________________

I thought about this for a while this weekend, cuz I’ve started to write a couple of stories with a similar premise multiple times. (Never finished any of them, cuz I’m lazy.)

The advice I gave 20 years ago, as the Web was just gaining a foothold in the economy as a viable vehicle for advertising, was very different from what I’d do today. 

Not because of AI…

… but because so many of the easily available resources back then are gone. 

* Newspaper want ads,

* Phone book yellow pages

* The lack of astonishment when you’d walk into an office unannounced

* The once-vibrant postal system... 

(And don’t get cocky, you younguns reading this. Today’s suggestions seem destined to be obsolete, too, in the near future. Probably much sooner than you think.)

The rapaciousness of the monopolies over media, the easily-triggered isolationist tendencies of average people, the lack of dealing with prospects face-to-face are all hard nuts to crack when you’re starting from scratch. 

And, as you suggest in your outline, a well-oiled network will always be your best resource, whether you’re going for a huge payday or just trying to eke out a living. 

Minus my reputation, my network of people who will take my phone calls, and my "screw you" money that allows me to pick and choose my projects…

… I’d frankly have to scramble. 

I don’t know if library computers allow you to use email, but I’d find out fast. 

And I’d look to move up Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs quickly, by finding safe shelter, with food, where I could stay alive while having good hygiene (so I didn’t get too shabby… cuz I’m gonna knock on doors). 

With skills, there are online joints like UpWork (and I think Fiverr is still around) where you can pitch yourself to people looking for copywriters. 

But you’d also need a place to accept and pay out money, which would require a smartphone. Or apps you can bring up on a library computer. (Fifty bucks isn’t enough to open a bank account.)

Plus, the online options are and will forever be changing often enough that any options available now could be gone tomorrow. 

Yes, these are those "interesting times" you’ve heard about. We’re living in them. 

So I’d do what I could with the restrictions you set out, which would be tough with no income and no home. 

I’m glad this is all hypothetical and I’m really not in this position anymore. 

But – and this is important – I could do it. If I had to. 

As long as I can wash up and groom myself and have clean clothes, I’d hit the pavement. 

Find places who use advertising — from bakeries to law offices, every joint that has a list to email or contact — and pitch myself as the guy who could ignite tired audiences and trigger sales. 

* Fire sales of existing inventory. 

* Special offers to wake up inactive customers. 

* Whatever could be mustered together for a fast turnaround.

Cuz it would probably be easier to explain that I’d do this magic for free…

… with only the guarantee that my ad would be emailed immediately, and I would be paid a low percentage of the haul. 

I would not trade my physical labor for money, unless it was essential. 

Halbert started his rebirth as a copywriting force, after being released from a scandalous vacation in Club Fed, by scoring a cheap hotel room and taking on dozens of quick jobs for very little money. 

Just blasted out ads one after the other, with no royalties or share in profit. 

And once he’d regained his balance, he shifted back to having an office and a high price tag. It took months, but he was on fire with ambition and drive. 

He even thought fondly about that period of time, because he was going after goals and knew in his heart he was gonna succeed. 

Paramount in any reverie about starting from scratch with zero resources…

… is the story behind your circumstances. 

It has to be honest, and believable…

… and able to be told quickly. 

You’re just setting the stage for why you’re offering magic for such small ROI…

… so you can spend most of your precious time (when you’ve scored a meet-up with any decision-maker, be it the head chef or the CEO) on persuading them to let you write a killer ad. 

I think the main things holding most people back, in such a fantasy, would be lack of confidence in pitching, and fear of confrontation or being told "No!"

Which is something I got over long ago. 

There’s lots more that pops into my head — including getting referrals to other biz or individuals who could use a rationally-explained access to magic, so your search for fresh clientele is shortcut — but the above is enough to see how I’d begin thinking about my survival. 

Much depends on computer access in the library today. And how to manage money in a world that no longer likes cash so much. 

And that damn smartphone that’s become an extension of our existence…

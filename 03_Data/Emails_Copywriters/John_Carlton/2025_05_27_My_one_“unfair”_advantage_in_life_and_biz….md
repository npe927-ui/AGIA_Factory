Howdy.

Want your career to take off to new heights?

Then live wild.

And take notes. (This is important).

A big part of my success boils down to the crazy adventures I survived before becoming a successful freelancer and consultant. 

Luckily for me, I come from a family of storytellers.

Relaying the delicious details of any adventure was second nature to me. 

Because you don't get away with dull stories in a family like mine.

This skill was my only unfair advantage as I got started down the path to copywriting success.

The love of a well-crafted story is a default setting in the human brain...

... but unfortunately, the skill of great storytelling is NOT.

If you wanna get good while enjoying the whopping pay checks that go along with that particular talent... 

... you have to work at it.

And you get good at telling stories by telling stories.

Big or small, doesn't matter. 

Here's a quick example:

I used to excel at talking my way past bouncers at clubs and
events.

I once waltzed my bad self into the venue of a sold-out Van Morrison concert.

With a cup of coffee in hand, I strolled up to the bouncer, looked him in the eye, and said, "This coffee is for Van."

The bouncer waved me through while everyone else waited outside.

Even I was stunned at my audacity and the success that followed. 

So much so that I just came clean and admitted the coffee wasn't actually for Van and left. 

It was just too easy at that point. 

Now what does any of this have to do with anything?

Everything.

Remember that good consulting is also a form of storytelling.

Your client suffers from a mishmash of complaints, problems, nightmares and quandaries...

...and none of it seems to make any sense.

However:

Everything makes sense with the right perspective.

But that perspective must be based on real facts and accurate data.

Rumors and gossip may be more fun, but they don't help anyone, not you or your client.

But the problem is...

... facts
and data are dead boring.

Unless you weave them into a compelling story.

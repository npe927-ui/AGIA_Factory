Hell, I sometimes tear up watching sappy television commercials. 

The fact that snarly, cynical guys have the biggest soft spots might crack you up, but it’s true.

Picture this:

It’s the early seventies in my college town. 

My fashion choices are questionable. (Don’t judge me. It's the 70s, bro). 

Anyway, a buddy asks for help coaching a kids’ baseball team.

Turns out, it’s the team that’s created for the kids no one else wants. 

The lousy players, the outcasts, the orphans of society.

* A couple kids who didn’t speak a word of English
* Several out-of-shape kids who couldn’t run to save their lives
* A few emotional basket cases who would burst into tears for no reason

And the only girl in the league. If this were Hollywood, she would have ended up the star player. 

But this is reality in the 1970s. And there are no rising stars in this group. 

Everyone else sees this team of misfits as perpetual losers…

As coaches, we are outcasts, too.

The others are all upstanding parents, still sporting their crewcuts from their glory days as star jocks. 

And then there’s my friend Bob and me — two long-haired hippies in torn jeans and “Up The Establishment” tee shirts. 

But we all share one thing: a love of baseball. 

The first practice is a disaster, just as predicted.

Kids showing up with decrepit gloves that fell apart with the first catch, the rampant crying and hurt feelings…

…  pure, unmitigated chaos. 

Being a nice guy isn’t working.

So screw it. 

I order the team to take a lap as punishment.

They get back, huffing and gasping…

…  and I make it clear that we were gonna do laps every time the whining starts. 

Cruel?

Nope. Here’s the thing:

I’m refusing to treat them like the misfits society has made them out to be. 

I’m treating them like REAL ball players.

And they LOVE it. 

It changes something deep in their psyche.

Suddenly, they start acting the part. 

Sure, we get blown out for the first half of the season.

The kids handle it like pros…

… and then, just when everyone (including Bob and me) thinks we’re out for the count… 

… the last game rolls around and we beat them by a run. 

The applause is thunderous, the kids are dancing with joy. 

It gets even better, too…

… in the second half of the season, we win most of our games. 

Not by enough to win the league. Like I said, this ain’t Hollywood. 

But the change in the kids is real, and that’s the true victory. 

I never saw any of those kids again after the season ended. 

I have no idea what became of any of them.

But I remember their faces. 

The point of all of this is to remember that you never know the effect you have on people. 

All you can do is try as hard as you can to change some things.

And take the little flashes of magic when you can.

If you can’t find the magic?

Then you work your butt off and create it. 

And it can turn up in the oddest places. 

So stay frosty, 

John

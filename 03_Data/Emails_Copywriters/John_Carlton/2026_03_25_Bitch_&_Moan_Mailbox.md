My assistant, Jane, and I share bragging rights for the concept. As for the title, I always have certain phrases and power words floating around my brain, and sometimes they wiggle up into consciousness and hang around until it’s obvious they belong.

As soon as I threw out the phrase "bitch and moan mailbag" (using mailbag cuz I grew up with clients who had actual mailbags arrive from the post office stuffed with actual stamped mail). Jane immediately yells "ooh, that’s good, I’m gonna write that down."

Getting to be creative (and a little naughty) is one of the main perks to being a published writer. And when I wrote my first book (Kick-Ass Copywriting Secrets of a Marketing Rebel), I vowed I would not allow censorship to get in the way of a good turn of phrase.

Which only solidified my determination to write my books and newsletters and blogposts and emails and websites like I have always wanted to: Just like I talk when around people I’m confident are adult enough to understand the power of "drunken sailor on shore leave" speech. 

Funny, outrageous, shock-worthy and most of all, 

**true**. 

My stuff is an acquired taste, and I’m sure I would have multiplied my take from all the piles of material I’ve written by quite a bit if I’d just gone "clean" with my prose.

Question Two: 

I figured you have(or had) a cat judging by the small cat figurine above the fireplace in one of your blog's pictures.

So herein lies my question:

How did you deal with her passing?

(4 days ago i had to put mine down after an excruciating (both for her and me - i was essentially a one-man hospice) battle with a brain tumour since Christmas. She would've turned 10 on the 3rd of june - and it pisses me off how she can get kidney failure and a brain tumour in a span of 6 months (forgot to mention but she also got diagnosed with early stage kidney failure during the summer), and how her life is done after not even 10 years.)

I'm kind of managing since i'm quite into philosophy and that helps somewhat... but i'm just curious/interested in another viewpoint.

______________________________________________________________________

Sorry for your loss. That really sucks. 

This'll be a long one, but I wanna answer your question as fully as I can.

I grew up with an unending lineup of domestic dogs and mostly-feral cats, which provided really intense insights on how to coexist with animals. Especially the sad days when your good furry buddy has to leave.

I learned so much from observing the cats in our neighborhood. No one ever neutered their pets, so there were very few "indoor" cats around, cuz they had shit to do every day. I learned about the miracle of birth from the feral Calico living in our garage…

… which had a litter of exactly nine kittens every spring, like clockwork. We called her Momma Cat, and she refused to let us pet her or feed her or play with her at all. 

(I did watch her mate with a Tom one night, which scared the bejesus out of me at the time… until I realized they weren’t fighting, and this was just the violent cat chapter in the "birds and the bees" rigamarole…)

But when she was ready to pop, she would allow us to set up a cardboard box next to the couch for her last couple of days of pregnancy. She actually pawed at the back door, and Mom had her all set up quickly. I watched over the little birthing box like a concerned doula, moved by the process but never shocked. 

We saw blood, guts and death on the regular in that neighborhood, which somehow seamlessly blended in with learning long division and reading books and getting kissed by classmates. My god, the cooties. 

Mama Cat lived for nearly a decade in the garage. Trusted us enough to occasionally accept help from her human hosts, and never held it against us for giving her kittens away — all nine of them, except when a tabby just connected with us. 

Cats, dogs, coyotes, raccoons, the occasional fox, rabbits, squirrels, gophers, lizards… the entire zeitgeist of Cucamonga at that time was like living in a Disney movie. Except the ones with teeth would occasionally snap at us, and they all ate each other with glee. 

Once I left home, I didn’t own pets at all, except when a girlfriend arrived with one. 

Then my wife entered the picture, and she brought four dogs and three cats with her. 

I don’t know what the hell I was thinking for the intervening years, not having good buddies around to keep the world at bay during most of my career. 

But I was happy to have the furry things back in my life again. 

There are dog people, and cat people, and combinations of the two. I don’t really trust anyone who can’t appreciate the glorious messiness of living with animals who bring dead mice home as gifts, and fight you for the best sleeping positions in bed, while forcing you to abandon black as a clothing option cuz they shed. 

Geez Louise, the shedding.

Still, there’s one thing I’ve learned after going through dozens of burials and final vet visits and — even worse — the sudden exit of a good buddy from an accident or cruel fast-moving bug. 

The lesson is this: You’re not allowed to have pets, if you aren’t prepared to 

**say "goodbye"** at some point. 

There will be unimaginable pain (I’m tearing up just thinking about holding Rosie on her last breath), and gut-punch memories for the rest of your time on this mortal coil. There’s no way around the heartbreak.

But that’s life. The love and camaraderie and unquestioned loyalty of every beast who — at some point over the millennia — decided to become pals of our arrogant, bloodthirsty, thoughtless and undeserving wing of the primate family tree can only be seen as a gift from nature. 

And because nature is a capricious and murderous overlord, most pets only live as long as a favorite tee shirt. No matter how much we pamper and care for them…

… and especially how much we come to rely on them being there when we need them. 

The universe is mostly hostile to every living creature and plant on this spinning orb. 

But there seem to be rules. 

Some of which we can figure out, and others we just have to create an elaborate sense of justice that helps us deal. 

I’m absolutely certain that Manfred, Rosie, Cooper, Lucy, Sara, Misha, Momma Cat and all the others will be awaiting me at the other end of that mysterious light when I bounce into the ether. Tails wagging, purrs purring like you can’t imagine, and everybody gets that special scritch that only ride-or-die buddies know about. 

You’re going to be fine. 

Deep breaths, long walks, maybe write your own obituary for the little fur ball. 

Continue to be a good person, live your life with gusto, and perhaps the notorious Cat Distribution Network will bring another deserving beast around to steal your heart one more time. 

You’re not being disloyal to the ones who leave. You won’t ever forget them. But others need a little of that love, too. 

This is how it all works. And always has. 

Now, if you’ll excuse me, dinner’s ready and I have to dry my sodden eyes before stumbling out into the other part of the house...

Question Three:

Do you prefer a Fender Strat to a Telecaster or a Gibson Les Paul, and WHY??

______________________________________________________________________

My first guitar was one of the shittiest stub-neck Strat copies that Vox ever made. Felt like a slab of wood ripped from a cheap couch and strung with cable wires, but the pickups were loud and I loved that little piece of shyte. 

Then I got a job washing dishes, and found a well-used ’64 Stratocaster in a pawn shop in San Bernardino for $125 in 1970, and I finally discovered what a good axe can do for your soul. 

Pacific blue, rosewood fingerboard, tone and volume dials from a Princeton Reverb, the name DINO pasted on the pick guard with silver letters, the telltale signs of a working musician everywhere (belt buckle scrapes on the back, knocks and pings from being handled aggressively onstage, slight cigarette burns on the headstock, worn frets, the whole menu).

She was so… 

**gorgeous**.

For years I hauled her around to gigs…

… until one day a suspicious guy offered me ten grand, in cash, on the spot, for her. I took her to a trusted shop and learned that her value had ballooned in the intervening years, thanks to Hendrix and Clapton. And I could no longer use her in public without inviting theft.

I was a Fender man, cuz I’d grown up near the original factory in Fullerton where Leo worked his magic. I’m pretty sure that Strat came straight from the shop, tracking the production numbers on the tail of the neck. You get more oomph from the Gibbies, but that Fender twang is unique (hand-wound pickups, mind you), and going through a Vox fuzz-wah gives you all the growl you’d ever want live. 

So I looked around for other guitars, and randomly plucked a nice used Esquire off a wall one day (a Japanese copy of the notorious ’62 Esquire, built to exact original specs)… and the little bastard just felt like a part of me from the first note played. I put in a neck lipstick-tube pickup, replaced the bridge pickup with a Seymour Duncan Hot Rail, put her through a Peavey tweed twin…

… and I was ready to put a band together for my fifth (and still the best) mid-life crisis. 

What a blast. 

What do you play?

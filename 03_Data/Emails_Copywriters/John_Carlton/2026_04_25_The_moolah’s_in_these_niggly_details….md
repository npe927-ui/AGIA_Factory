Notice what no one else cares about...Howdy.

Some marketing and copywriting techniques are formulaic. 

If you’re sticking to a straightforward structure, you can get your chatbot of choice to spit out sort of decent copy in about 5 seconds. 

(The thought would have boggled my mind in the heyday of those direct response years, when I frantically scribbled my brainfarts on napkins at airport bars before nearly missing yet another flight. But enough about me in those dinosaur years.)

From what I hear, the results from AI-generated copy have been mediocre at best – unless it’s heavily - and I mean HEAVILY - edited by a human. 

That’s where storytelling comes in. 

And this, folks, is where so many people struggle to captivate an audience. 

If that sounds like you, you’re in luck, cuz I’m about to pull back the curtain and let you in behind the scenes. 

As a guideline, here are three simple rules to make sure you don’t bore your reader to sleep:
* You must involve the listener.  

* A good story has inherent interest. (Shock value is even better.) 

* Finally, have a point. A good narrative opens up new avenues of conversation and thought. 

Your story can be long or short. 

But no matter what, it MUST draw the reader in with imagery and emotion. 

EXAMPLE:

Here’s how I once described a hotel I was staying at in Hollywood:
______________________________________________________________________

“I’m standing on the balcony of this famous old hotel with the wild rock and roll history, soaking up the balmy Southern California evening, sipping a beer and watching the developing street dramas seven floors down on the Sunset Strip.

It’s quite a show, too.  

Earlier, some friends tried to drag me into the Comedy Store next door to see if Chris Rock would pull another surprise visit to fine-tune his act for his upcoming Academy Awards MC gig (as he had for the previous three nights).  

But who needs prepared scripts when you’re smack in the middle of a real-life Fellini movie like West Hollywood?

This part of town is where every kid and hustler with a dream of “making it in showbiz” lands first when they hit the coast. 

The scenes playing out below are what Guns ‘n’ Roses sang about in Welcome To The Jungle: “…you can get anything you want, but you better not take it from me.”  (Also “…if you got the money, honey, we got your disease…”)

From my perch, I can see the farmboy from Kansas putting the make on the little princess from Rio, who’s being introduced to the failed producer from San Diego by the strung-out guitar player from Belfast… 

… and each of them appears to have a mangled screenplay in their back pocket.

This town is like a black hole -- it sucks you in, no matter how far away you are when you first hear the siren call of fame and the stars start twinkling in your eyes.  

And, like a real black hole, it will gobble you up.”

______________________________________________________________________

Note the vivid details and references. 

It requires paying attention to stuff no one else notices. 

Beginning storytellers, in the same joint, with access to the same information, might say something like:

“Yeah, I had a room in a decent hotel, a fifty buck taxi ride from the airport. I guess it was some kinda famous place or something at one time.”

Just as bad, of course, is the unfocused babbler.  

You know the ones. 

“Did I tell you about the UFO that attacked us? No? It was Tuesday last week… no, wait, it was Wednesday. Yeah, it must have been Wednesday, because I was headed to IHOP to meet Suzy for waffles — you know they have specials every Wednesday, don’t you…”

Details matter, but not the boring details or drifting tangents no one cares about. 

It’s tough, and you gotta spend some time thinking about what your audience finds intriguing. 

Well, hey, if it were easy, everyone would do it right? 

But you’re not just anyone, you’re a real writer not relying on a few lazy prompts. 

So grab your laptop, tablet, or old school pen and paper (they still make those, right?)

And get busy crafting your eye-popping riveting tale.

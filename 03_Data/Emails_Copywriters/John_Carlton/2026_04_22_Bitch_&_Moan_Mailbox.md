Question #2
Have you ever been much of a fiction reader? If so, did that have any impact on your copy?

______________________________________________________________________

Yes. I devoured most of the “required hippie reading” back in college — Dune, Stranger In A Strange Land, all of Kerouac (and huge slices of the other Beats), Henry Miller, Bukowski, Hemingway, Dashiell Hammett, Vonnegut, Bradbury, Asimov…

… and moved on to more contemporary writers as I stumbled into adulthood. Stephen King and Hunter S Thompson were very influential in my own growth as a writer. 

Being a well-rounded reader is an absolute requirement for anyone serious about becoming a writer. 

Along with both fiction and non-fiction books, I plow through the New Yorker regularly along with a large helping of trusted news sources. 

And, of course, massive helpings of non-fiction.

But all that was mostly about stuffing my brain with raw material. 

The single most influential book, for me, was The Elements Of Style, by E. B. White. 

That quick read got me to stop relying on adjectives, and instead to choose better verbs… 

… while keeping my prose sparse and short and punchy. 

That’s where my insistence on "Power Words" came from. (You can still get that report as a bonus when you get your grubby hands on Kick Ass Copywriting Secrets https://m190.isrefer.com/go/kacs/jcemail/, by the way. If you haven’t grabbed your copy already, stop slacking.)

The biggest effect on my writing, though, was simply the arrival of the Internet. 

I was no longer constrained by space — I could write 30-page sales messages, if I thought that’s what was needed to make a sale. 

But it also let me air everything out, cuz space is free online. 

So I started writing single sentence paragraphs, and breaking up any complex thought with ellipses (…) and paragraph breaks and bullets. 

Clarity ensued.

Pretty sure I was one of the first to really air copy out like that. My colleagues were often still writing for clients who mailed pieces or published print ads, and were slower to abandon that style of limited space.

Ultimately, though, a good writer must find their own “voice”.

Which becomes their main tool for communication and influence.

Question #3

How can I create a unique, memorable and enjoyable-to-read cover letter to stand out among the other job applicants? (Without any past experience and client.)

______________________________________________________________________

You’re actually writing a sales letter. Selling you. You (and your skills) are the product.

So you do what you’d do with any sales piece:

* Stuff your brain with deep research and as much solid info on the demographics of your audience (which may be just a single person).

* Figure out where their pain points are. 

To do that, ask lots of questions: 

* Why are they hiring? 

* Did someone quit? 

* Does that happen often? 

* Are they expanding, going into new territory, being bought out, or what?

If they’re being coy about who is actually doing the hiring (“You’ll be interviewed by HR”, or “Our staffing specialists…”) then do some detective work. 

I often tell the story of my early jobs, where I would make friends with the receptionist. 

Just be extra friendly (but NOT flirty), maybe offer them a piece of gum. Just acknowledging their existence goes a long way.

No one else in the company gets ignored like they do. And yet, they see almost everything that passes through the doors, and hears ALL the good gossip.

If you’re forced to go through the phone or email or text, do your best to find out who you’re communicating with. 

Without being obnoxious, find out what you can. 

I’m serious about the detective work, you know. Be a spy. 

Order something through the company, and find out how they handle customers. Use their webpages, all the way through to the final sale. 
Follow every link possible, see what happens. 

You may find a major flaw somewhere, which you can discuss later to impress them with your thoroughness. Then check out the competition, see where they stand in the hierarchy of their cutthroat market niche. 

If you’re brave, you can attach something to your “letter”… if it’s an actual letter that gets delivered in an envelope. I used to staple money to the top of the first page, per the Gary Halbert “grabber” tactic. Gave me something to talk about in the critical first paragraph.

I also got a lot of traction with stapling lottery tickets to the letter. And then opening with something like “Hey, I’ve just maybe earned you a million dollars here…”

But the tactics are so much less important than the research. 

I noticed you say “Without any past experience and client” in your question. 

I highly doubt just having a clever cover letter is going to get past inexperience, unless they’re specifically touting a job that requires nothing. 

Still, it’s good experience just going through this entire process. Treat it like your life depended on it — what would you do if that were the case? You’d put as much good stuff in your corner as you could muster — info, inside intel, actual experience dealing with the company (even if you just go by and visit the joint, if they’re not completely digital).

The attitude is: There is a way around ANY challenge. 

You may not like or want to use the best ways to win… and I hope you would immediately toss any idea that is illegal or unethical. 

But there IS a way to meet this situation, and get the gig. The top freelancers already understand most of the ways, because they’ve been meeting and conquering these issues their entire career. 

If you’re a rookie, it’s time to get your feet wet. 

Let us know what you do, and how it goes.

Question #4

I am from India and am looking to get my first dental implant client. But my English is average and I am terrified of sales calls. Can you give me some advice?

______________________________________________________________________

Why are you afraid of sales calls? 

Being shy isn’t a valid reason. Most of the top writers I know are introverts, like me. And we’ve all found ways to get past it. 

I took a shortcut, and read the classic “How To Win Friends And Influence People” self-help book, by Dale Carnegie. It’s jam-packed with good advice for even the most cowardly confrontation-avoidant introvert. 

You can read that book in a weekend. 

* Take notes. 

* Read it again. 

* Start using the techniques in everyday life — you’ll be stunned how effective they are.

Still, “average English” is fine for anything regarding sales or ads you will ever want to do. We touched on this in another question from the mailbag, earlier. 

You’re not alone in being shy. 

But now that I’ve blown your excuses by giving you a solid (and proven) solution…

… you have no reason to be in that camp anymore. 

Get going. And good luck.

Question #5

Your quote (from the last Bitch & Moan session) got me to thinkin' ...

Halbert started his rebirth as a copywriting force, after being released from a scandalous vacation in Club Fed, by scoring a cheap hotel room and taking on dozens of quick jobs for very little money.

Sounds like a great setup for a short story... 

Reminded me of Sam Shepard's play Geography of a Horse Dreamer: Cody can dream the winner of horse races. Bad guys hold him captive, tied to a bed in a sleazy hotel room somewhere, taking advantage of his prescient ways. 

Ever try playwriting? Also, anywhere we can get our mitts on any of your fiction writing?

______________________________________________________________________

I hung out with what I called “The Hollywood D-List Crowd” for several years in the 80s. Mostly for the parties, but I was also curious about how screenwriters (and writers in general) fared in that weirdo land. 

I quickly learned that most “writers” in LA were just like most of the actors who were really waitresses or bartenders. They were pursuing the dream, and good for them. 

But most of them weren’t doing it very efficiently. 

They all imagined there was a plot line in their lives that would magically place them in the right place, at the right time…

… and everything wonderful would flow from that moment. 

Perhaps it happened once in a while. Quentin Taratino’s producer was hanging out in that crowd — this was before Pulp Fiction hit it big, and Quentin was still mostly working at Video Archives as a clerk. 

Then everything changed for them, and we never saw that producer dude again. 

But it wasn’t a magic moment. QT had been pounding out screenplays for a decade in his spare time. And even had a couple optioned, and some were made into low-grade movies. 

The thing is, he was working the process. 

Nearly all the “writers” I hung out with had ONE screenplay they’d written, and were busy the rest of the time trying to “set up a meeting with John Candy’s people”. 

I was astonished. Those screenplays were often just awful. They were, and read like, first drafts of the first screenplay they’d ever attempted. 

And I’m pretty you don’t usually get masterful on the first try at anything. Ever.

So when I was offered a gig at a writer’s table on a sitcom…

… I politely said no. 

Same with fiction. 

I wrote a couple of novels, as quickly as I could. 

I always liked what John D. MacDonald said — that you don’t get good until after you’ve written ten books. 

So he pounded out ten in his first year. And started making money with his Travis McGee mysteries soon after. 

But then I met some working novelists who weren’t John D. Mac Donald. 

They had multiple books under their belt that had been published. They had an agent. They could find at least one or two of their books in a Barnes & Noble, sometimes. 

And I realized I earned more from writing ONE ad than they earned in a year. 

And that was the NORM for the industry. You’ve got your main authors who take up all the slots at the airport bookshelves. 

And if you ain’t Stephen King or one of the romance novelists du jour (most of whom are men, by the way), then you ain’t earning a decent living. 

So I took Elmore Leonard’s advice: “Any writer is a fool who doesn’t do it for the money.”

And I shelved my novels and short stories, and have been happily writing copy ever since. 

Maybe after I’m gone, some of the fiction will leak out. 

But probably not. I could feel that I was reaching a mastery of it, but knowing the reality of the dream soured it for me. 

(I was also a cartoonist, you know. Had weekly strips in both my high school and college newspapers for years and years. My style predated Doonesbury, and it may have lead to another career also.) 

But no. 

I’ve chosen the noble craft of copywriting, and it has brought me so much joy and so many adventures, that I cannot even imagine having gone on a different path. 

And by the way… I tried to help many of my pals who were dead broke “writers” by dragging them, kicking and screaming, into the world of advertising. 

Only one ever did the necessary work to get good. And he did very well. 

The others never got past the first project I gave them. Too hard, too uncomfortable dealing with clients, too…

Yeah. I’m all for going after “the dream”. 

But in my thirties, my dream morphed into just earning a living at whatever kind of writing I could find. 

And I found direct response long-copy style marketing, and the dream went wild.

Chapter 1:

Definition of Financial and Location Independence

What do you do?

Where do you live?

Where are you from?

These are common questions that you get asked when you are traveling, if only because the other member of the discussion can only summon this level of English to ask these basic questions. Unfortunately, while I have a definitive answer to the first, it is very rarely understood without extensive explanation. If I answer the second and third, I sound like frumpy hitchhiker/gypsy, or maybe a student on a gap year. In the Philippines people decided I was a "webmaster" and in Thailand people usually think I teach English like most other farang (what they call foreigners). Everyone has their own interpretation.

Imagine if you had total freedom of time and location. If you had no compelling reason beyond your own goals and desires to live anywhere in particular. If you only spent your time on projects that you decided to pursue. This is the dream, right? Well, you are in luck. These chapters will detail how this isn't some unrealistic dream, but rather a path you can legitimately pursue. And while it certainly takes a bit of work, it's easier than you think.

Now let's define some terms to get on the same page.

Location Independence:the ability to permanently sustain oneself without geographical anchors.

Note: this does NOT mean traveling for 3 months on savings until you run out of money!

The worst part of a conventional job is having to go to the office every morning at 8 am. I have soul-sucking memories of being constantly stuck in traffic on the way to a cubicle I hated with a passion. How can one live up to their full potential like that?

At a minimum level, to achieve total location independence, which frees you to pursue indefinite travel, work from home, or live from whatever city you'd like, the formula is quite simple. Money made > money spent, conditionally dependent upon zero in-person requirements.

Lucky you. There is this emerging trend where more and more companies are allowing their employees to work from wherever, at least on some level. Great! If you 1) currently work for one of these companies, and 2) have ONLY the goal of freeing yourself up geographically, then you can probably stop reading here. You'll still continue to trade hours for dollars on some level (tsk-tsk, you time-prostitute you) but you'll have at least accomplished one level of freedom. Boom. Done.

But what if you can't do that, if your company is old-fashioned? Or if you are unemployed (or newly-employed)? What if you don't want to rely on someone else giving you your paycheck? Better yet, how do you want to develop business assets that generate income not tied to your time? Keep reading.

Financial Independence:the ability to generate income greater or equal to living expenses with no direct time-for-money trade.

Most ultra-rich individuals don't need to continue to work, though they usually do. This is not because having a million dollars in the bank means you can gradually spend that and it won't run out before you die (it might, but let's not gamble with lifespans and inflation here). Cool fact: once you have a certain size bank account, the interest on it pays all your living expenses indefinitely - you could live to be 200 years old and at the end of your life the bank account would still have the same amount left.

Some projections actually indicate that if you make 3x your required income level (assuming some level of consistency) for 10 years, after that you can "retire" off the return on the savings you've accumulated, and maintain that same income level without working.

Some assumptions about you: I am guessing you probably 1) don't have millions of dollars nor want to wait to become financially independent until you do, and 2) don't want to make triple your required income and then wait 10 years. Good, me neither.

Breaking news! There is a much quicker way to achieve financial independence. There are many ways you can work for yourself and achieve location independence, and some of these business models also grant financial independence as well. The author living off revenues from past books, the blogger, the dropshipper, the app creator, the founder of a content marketing agency, even those channels on youtube that you watch when you should be working. Each of these individuals makes consistent income based on the existing systems or following they've created.

Now I know what you are thinking. But Grant, bloggers still have to blog, dropshippers still have to dropship, and youtubers still have to youtube! Yes indeed, and the mega-millionaire might have to spend an hour a week keeping an eye on their portfolio. But let me ask you this: do any of these individuals have an hourly rate? Do they have a salary paid based on working full-time or even half-time? Would they still make a sustainable income if they took a week, or even a month-long vacation? If they are doing things right, then they've created sustainable systems which grant them financial independence, and the answers to those questions are exactly what we want them to be.

Your Turn

It's time to chart your journey towards location and financial independence. Chances are you will do this in a two-step transition. The reality is you will likely achieve location independence first, then financial independence will follow as you gain experience and refine your business. For example, one strategy which will be discussed is how freelancing can be a good starting point or transition, and is something most financially and location independent entrepreneurs have utilized at some point. This is an example of having achieved location independence without financial independence.

But, even from the very beginning, you will be working for yourself. No matter what happens to the economy, or your old company, you will make a sustainable, flexible income that frees you up to live in the way you choose.

First off, how much do you spend per month right now? How much do you make? The budget that the average American lives on is $4,258 (as recently as 2012) according to the U.S. government. This is a budget that you can translate easily to almost every city in the world. The most interesting aspect about being location independent is that once you achieve this status you can automatically start spending less money. As a quick example, if you work from home, what happens to your monthly gas payment? How much more will you end up eating at home versus at restaurants?

But those aren't very exciting examples. Instead, consider how the average American spends over $9,000 per year on transportation - car payments, gas, etc., and this doesn't even include car insurance. Now imagine walking down the street in Berlin to a nearby coffee shop, or grabbing a Tuk-Tuk in Bangkok for the equivalent of 3 dollars. Ironically, once you start traveling most people end up spending LESS on transportation.

If you think about those surreal, exotic travel destinations on your list, chances are you will automatically become richer just by going there. Chapter3:

Freedom Math - Making Enough to Escape

The next step is to show you how you can make enough to escape! Let's pick Prague as an example to see how we can do this. As a mid-range option that's on several lists of the Top 25 Cities in the World to visit, it's not a bad starting point.

Let's start from scratch with a more "comfortable" budget by adding an extra 30% to the projections in the previous chapter. If you look back at the budget chart you will find that you can live on $1,133 in Prague, but maybe you do enjoy the occasional fine dining, or maybe you want to invest in lessons to learn to speak Czech. So let's add on 30% as a buffer - $1,133 x 130% = $1472.90. For simplicity let's round up to $1,500. While you can probably live several months at this level on your current savings, the goal is tonot require the use of yoursavings. Ever. In fact your savings should be growing while you are traveling if you follow what you learn in this book.

Let's go through a variety of business models you can create which generate this income, with a variety of options in terms of time spent, freedom, and knowledge required. No matter your level or experience, at least one of the options below will apply to you:

Required income - $1,500

1. Freelancing (low skill requirement but tied to your time)

a.Part time work - 20 hours per week at $19/hour

b.Full time work - 40 hours per week at $9.50/hour

2. Service Business (Content marketing, SEO, brand management, email marketing, web development - some previous experience required)

a.Medium-low end clients - 3 clients at $500/client

b.Medium high end clients - 1-2 clients at $1,000+/client

3. Product Businesses (knowledge or research abilities required)

a.Ebooks (poorly launched) - 8 short ebooks selling $200/month per book

b.Ebooks (decently well written/launched) - 4 ebooks at $400+/book

c.Udemy video courses - 3 Udemy courses at $500+/course (based on Udemy's stated instructor earnings)

d.Dropshipping or Amazon Products - 1-2 sites or products at ~$1,000 each

Keep in mind these are MINIMUM requirements to show you how you are much closer than you think to the kind of freedom that enables you to pursue what interests you,without being tied to a paycheck. In reality, this is a first step which is easily attainable, and from there you can scale or diversify. For example, I have several entrepreneur friends who make 6 figures from ebooks, make triple this Prague budget from ONE dropshipping site, and I make several multiples of it from my Udemy courses and also have much more than 3 clients in my service business. But the first step for each of us was getting to this minimum level, then growing our businesses to the next level. Now each of us makes more than we did back in the States,while spending less and living better because of the freedom of our lifestyle.

Chapter4:

What You're Afraid of and Why it's Wrong

What happens if I break my leg?

Run out of money? Lose all my clients?

What if people realize I don't know what I'm doing?

What do I do when I get lonely and miss my family/friends/goldfish? What if no one speaks English?

Where will I buy that special brand of conditioner that smells like cinnamon and strawberries?!

…What if I fail?

Once a dream starts to become a reality, inevitably there is a moment where the abstract desire for something starts to become more concrete, and along with the details typically comes lots of fear and even more what-ifs.

Don't worry, we all go through this. But here is some help from the perspective of being on the other side of things. Typical fears tend to fall into a couple categories:

---

## Category 1: Business or Financial Failure

"The only failure is not to try" - George Clooney

Fear of failure is one of the most common and undermining fears. And it is almost always unfounded. You are embarking on a new adventure, in business and in life, and with this comes lots of ambiguity and uncertainty, which gives rise to fears of failure. You've got that $1,500 budget - now what? What if something happens and you lose all your clients, or your accounts get pirated, or you get sued? Let me tell you a secret: one of my friends who makes $50,000+ per month has the same fears. That should show you how unrelated to reality it is. Last week I got threatened with a lawsuit. A couple months ago another entrepreneur friend lost most of his clients (and 80% of his revenue) in one week.

These kinds of things happen, but here's the kicker -- they really aren't that big of a deal. They are just minor setbacks on the way to something greater.

Sure, it's inconvenient to lose clients, but if you got them in the first place you can surely get more. In a freak accident, your million-dollar business gets destroyed.

1)Well, keep in mind you are lucky you had one of those to lose in the first place

2)There is always some salvageable part of the business, experience, or client-base

3)You built one and you can build another, and this time bigger, faster, better

I spoke with a friend last week who is doing insanely well in his online business, and yet he keeps his day job despite wanting desperately to travel. Why? Exactly the way he put it:

What if I fail?

This is a guy who can afford at least a year of nonstop travel based on his current savings, even if he lost 100% of his income tomorrow.

In that unlikely situation, even then he could replicate his own success using the same process that led to his current success. It might set him back a month or two, but even then he would be traveling and living a great lifestyle. If you are just getting started and don't have much experience, then at the very least you can take solace in the fact that even with minimal experience, if your first project fails, you lose all your income, and are about to run out of savings, you can ALWAYS fall back on some simple freelancing until you get back up and running again. I've had rates for as much as $50 per hour for freelancing 6 months after I graduated college, and even with an hourly rate of $10 you'll never have to pack up and "go home". Unless you want to visit all your old friends back home that are slowly suffering I-hate-my-job/boss/life-death.

---

## Category 2: Access to Healthcare, Comfort Products, Clean Water, etc.

You might be gluten-free, allergic to dairy, diabetic, have IBS, or be hopelessly dependent on contacts to see. It's ok. The access to the things you need are available even in extremely underdeveloped countries. In fact, "underdeveloped" is a relative term, and these countries will probably surprise you.

For example, the Philippines has terrible infrastructure, in so many different ways, as any Filipino will agree. Yet I got LASIK eye surgery in Manila. I don't like to eat rice, and yet have spent a lot of time Asia. Is it inconvenient sometimes? Sure, but no more than trying to be gluten-free while eating at Subway by getting the salad instead of the sandwich. Doable if it's important to you? Without a doubt.

This was something I was concerned about when I first set off to travel. I had never been much of anywhere, so I didn't know much beyond the American stereotypes of what other countries are like. A friend from Zimbabwe put it best: "You know how everyone just thinks of Africa as a desert? You see this grass and these trees? Yeah, it's basically just like this where I am from."

But what if the water isn't clean to drink?! Yes, this is something to be aware of, especially in places like India. In reality, it is pretty easy to work around this issue. Drink only bottled water if you are concerned - basically every country on Earth has bottled water everywhere - and carry some probiotics and Loperamide. About once every 6-12 months you might eat something bad and be out of commission for a day or two, but that's only slightly more often than it happens back home anyways.

And finally, when it comes to beauty and comfort products, almost every culture likes these kinds of things, so while the exact types might be different, you'll be able to get what you need (or order it from abroad from places like iherb.com). In fact, some people in underdeveloped countries actually use MORE of these types of products than you do. Hard to believe? Come see for yourself.

## Category 3: Social Fears

Out of all the fears people tend to have, I'd say this one is what most people should be concerned with, however probably not for the reasons you'd think. Inevitably when you leave the safety of friends and family, there is a sense of insecurity that often develops. Despite this you can instantly message, FaceTime, tweet or email people back home, you are still setting out among a sea of strange faces (at least at first) and this can be a challenge. Any feelings of loneliness are going to be overridden by the excitement of new experiences and new places for a time, but come Christmas when you are going to the beach in tropical weather on the other side of the planet…it can feel a bit lonely.

All of that is not what I would warn you against, however. In fact this forces you to get out of your comfort zone, and to connect with the people around you, even if they don't speak English! This can be a beautiful thing. Even the biggest barriers of the most foreign cultures can be broken with just a touch of open-mindedness.

The real concern for most people is losing their social anchors. Accountability and social influence are powerful forces, and these dynamics instantly change the moment you set foot abroad. Are you accustomed to being surrounded by type A entrepreneur types to kick you in the butt to keep moving forward? You'll have to find a way to maintain that drive and focus amidst tourists looking to do nothing but party in Amsterdam, or the countless retired white guys who just sit around drinking San Miguel beer all day in the Philippines.

Most people struggle with maintaining motivation and focus even in traditional settings, teased away from their important projects by facebook bleeps and alluring YouTube videos of that guy who messes up a backflip off that house. Now imagine you are in a place where everything isnew. There is new alien-looking fruit to try, ancient ruins to see, mind-blowing natural splendor that is like nothing you've ever seen! But you still have to get up early and put in focused work before you go have fun.

Here are some suggestions about how to not let your business slide while on the road:

1.Plug into a group of motivated people (particularly entrepreneurs) wherever you go. There are plenty of tools for doing this. I personally usewww.dynamitecircle.comandwww.nomadlist.io.

2.Create a small group, weekly accountability call to keep yourself (and them) on track.

3.Understand that your business will likely stagnate, or at best stay consistent, while you are more actively traveling.Liveabroad (as opposed to doing the frantic tourist thing) for longer periods of time to actuallygrowyour business. I'll spend at least 3 months in one location sometimes when I'm in "work" mode.

4.Put in your 4 hours before lunch. Get focused work out of the wayeverymorning. 8 hours scattered throughout the day is nowhere near as effective as 4 focused hours completed before the interruptions start to creep into your day. Make it an ironclad habit. You'll get more done, but work less and have more fun time to explore or go on adventures. Note: it's important to get up a bit early and do this in the morning, even though you can wake up whenever you want now! Inevitably, come lunchtime, the never-ending interruptions begin.

5.Reflect every night on what you are working towards. The thing I struggled with most on the road is an odd sense of having accomplished my biggest life goal. Immediately afterwards, I had this nagging question of "now what"? I was not prepared to answer that question! Should I just shoot for some arbitrary level of high income? Should I try to travel more to just dosomething? For a while I ignored the question, but when you do that the answer tends to creep in from your environment. Your purpose in life should not be something that is passively accepted from others! I have seen many who begin traveling getting swept up in their surroundings because they lost sight of what they needed to do for themselves. Usually they end up somewhere they regret: a failed business, running out of money, or just becoming aimless. Once this began to happen to me, I spent a lot of time reflecting and revisiting my dreams and goals and created an updated path forward in life that I found extremely inspiring. Immediately everything became much more clear, stable, and exciting. I had reestablished internal anchors for myself, and had a new purpose.

"When a man does not know what harbor he is making for, no wind is the right wind."-Seneca

Chapter5:

The 3 Types of Mobile Businesses

Let's get you started on your first location-independent business.

There are a million ways to make a million dollars, the issue is figuring out which way is best for you. That's why this book isnotabout how to start your first business doing X. Because no matter what X is, chances are good that it would actually not be a good fit for more people.

In this chapter, we will be going through different business model options to allow you to get started in the best way possible, with the highest chance for success. Since you are reading this book, that means you likely are interested in businesses that grantlocation independence. Great, that narrows it down. Even beyond location independence, you will likely want to work towardsfinancial independencein the long term as well. So as we discuss some good starting business models, you will want to keep these goals in mind. Not all of them will grant financial independence, especially not right away, but all are stepping stones in that direction.

---

## The 3 Main Categories of The Mobile Business

1.The Freelancer or Consultant

This category creates location independence but typically not financial independence - there is a relatively direct work to income relationship. This is a good starting point for many. I am a big fan of the philosophy of just getting started however you can, and the rest will follow. When you go too big, or try to choose the 100% arbitrarily"best"option, that's where most people fail, give up, or just never end up getting started. That's why I give the suggestion of freelancing to most people who ask how to start their own business and travel the world. It's not what most people picture (things are never as sexy as you picture when you are just getting started) but this model has the lowest barrier to entry.

Advantages

1.Minimal to no previous experience required

2.Can get started extremely quickly

3.Payoff is almost immediate

Disadvantages

1.Still likely trading time for money

2.Not scaleable

3.Low ceiling on earning potential in most cases

If you want to get started as fast as possible, or have little previous experience, freelancing is likely your best option. Let's explore some additional models that you will likely use, if not as your first business, then later when you"graduate"from freelancing.

2. The Packaged/Productized Service

A step or two beyond freelancing, this model provides some kind of service in such a way that it is sold like a product. In this part of the spectrum you will find agencies (marketing agencies, development agencies, etc.) as well as SaaS (software as a service companies) as well as some others. To understand the significance of this model, let's look at the common transition from freelancer to Productized Service provider:

Hypothetical Harry is a medium-experience web developer. He quits his desk job and begins freelancing. He starts getting sick of working directly for clients - it feels too much like a job and he has basically maxed his earning potential. He has gotten a good feel for what the primary value behind his service is and decides to package it up and hire a team to fulfill it.

Previous Model: Freelancing for $50/hr

Previous Cap: 160 hours or $8,000 per month (although usually much lower)

New Model:

$2,000 per Yoga for Babies industry website creation, plus

$400 per month for ongoing maintenance

New Cap (with hired team to actually do the work):

4 new clients per month - $8,000

20 ongoing maintenance clients - $8,000

His margin is roughly 50% after costs

Total New Cap on Profits: $8,000

On the surface it appears to be the same, but here's the kicker: he's not actually doing the work now, AND he can easily expand his team as he gets more clients to dramatically increase the cap on his earnings. Marketing and Development Agencies, run by everyday entrepreneurs, regularly generate millions of dollars per year once they become well established.

But, like every model, this one has it's pros and cons:

Advantages:

1.Much higher earning potential than freelancing

2.Once the systems are in place and the team is hired, it can be less work

3.Can get started on a reasonable timeline, but scaling can be stressful and takes time

Disadvantages:

1.Very difficult without previous experience in the industry or as an entrepreneur

2.If you are transitioning from freelancing to a Productized Service model, you will earnlessand workmoreat first, while you are training your team and creating business systems

3.Many more variables involved - sales, customer support (aka hand-holding), marketing funnels, managing the team, business systems/standard operating procedures, etc.

The Productized Service model can be an exciting and lucrative option for entrepreneurs or those with some experience in a particular industry. However, this is less of a one-size-fits-all model, so make sure to thoroughly understand and evaluate your willingness to deal with each element of this model. Don't worry, we'll be discussing this in more detail in the next chapter.

3. The Product Model

The"Product Model"can actually refer to a wide variety of businesses, as"product"is pretty loosely defined here. This could be a Wordpress plugin, or a selfie stick sold on Amazon. The main idea is that it is some kind of digital OR physical product, and generally this type of business has the earning potential.

Realistically, while service businesses can indeed scale, it can be a difficult process, and there is a functional limit on scaling in most cases. This the difference between a Walmart and the highest earning consultant ever, or between the most profitable marketing agency on Earth and Amazon. While each business pulls in impressive numbers, there is still an order of magnitude of difference between the service-based businesses and Walmart or Amazon. Some product businesses even surpass the economies of small countries!

But this scalability comes with some drawbacks as well. Let's look at the pros and cons of this model to see if it's right for you:

Advantages:

1.MASSIVE scalability in most cases

2.If set up in the correct way, this can be the most passive business model

Quick side note on the definition of"passive income/businesses":

Passive income refers to revenue generated by some asset or business that has been set up to run almost completely autonomously. Beyond the initial work (which can be quite substantial) very little ongoing work is required to maintain the asset, creating a situation where you can get paid without working. This creates total financial and location independence once the profit covers your expenses! Thus,passive incomeis preferable toactive incomeif there is sufficient financial runway and skills to pull it off.

Disadvantages:

Chapter 6:

The Criteria for Choosing Your First (or Next) Business

Now that you've read (and hopefully studied) the pros and cons of each model, you might find yourself drawn to one particular model or another based on your tolerance for risk. Risk-tolerance is a huge factor in determining which model you should start with, however there are a couple other factors which are even more important!

Criteria #1 - Your Timeline

I want you to take a moment and reflect on the goals and the timeline of your pursuit of location and financial independence, in both the short-term and the long-term.

1. Are you just getting started with few skills or experience, and without much savings to buy you time? Freelancing is almost certainly the best option.

2. Is your primary concern quitting the job you hate right now and getting started as fast as possible? Then a freelance or service business model is likely the right option for you.

3. Are you determined to achieve ultimate financial freedom as fast as possible, while willing to sacrifice short-term flexibility, and have the resources of time and capital at your disposal? Then a high-earning service or product business might be a good option for you.

Since there are dramatic differences in the starting timelines of each model, the best option often will just end up falling on how much time you have, or can afford to buy. If you've just lost your job last week, and have $1,000 dollars in the bank, you would obviously NOT want to start a product business - you'd end up living in your parent's basement before your business was profitable!

Freelancing and service businesses can be profitable almost overnight if done correctly, although service businesses can take longer. As a rule of thumb, you can expect product businesses to takeminimum2 months to get started.

Criteria #2 - Your Skill

Despite what is marketed by countless gurus, you cannot always just buy the latest info product and figure out how to start any kind of business without any previous knowledge experience. Or at least, you shouldn't! If we all have ten years to figure things out, of course we could start pretty much any business imaginable and develop all the required skills. But unfortunately this is unrealistic, and depending on where you start, you might fail by the very nature that it takes too long to develop the skills and experience to succeed! You might run out of savings, or run out of motivation.

I recently had a friend ask me for business advice about a travel agency she wanted to start. Her question:"People keep telling me to learn how to create a Wordpress website to get started - what's the best way to do this?"My advice was to not start that way at all, and to stop taking business advice from those people! It was clear that they were not likely successful entrepreneurs by the very nature of that advice. Why is that? Well, conventional entrepreneur wisdom would dictate that she should get started providing the service to people ASAP to iron out the kinks. But instead this advice was the equivalent of saying"No you need to learn how to build websites and do online marketing first."

Now, she very well could have used that inadvisable approach, but after 3 months of teaching herself how to make a halfway decent Wordpress site, how many paying customers would she have? Likely answer: zero. She'd then probably get told it was time to learn about guest posting to get traffic. Then opt-in bribes, email marketing, and blogging. Then PPC, video sales letters, and on and on…

Instead, I advised her that she should just get started providing the actual service to people, and she could easily skip the website for now. Her business timeline (considering the lack of previous experience) should look like this:

1)Start as a freelance travel consultant, plugging into your current networks as well as relevant organizations that would be interested in your services

2)Use that experience to get testimonials, referrals, and a refined offering of your service. THEN create avery basic one-page websitewith packages you offer. NOT on Wordpress, as that is a complicated platform to learn, and you don't need that kind of functionality. You now have transitioned from consultant to a service business.

3)FINALLY, once you've scaled that sufficiently, then develop a brand and a following. It might be a viable option to build out a platform that might provide customized services, blogging and being an affiliate for travel products, or creating info product versions of your service. You've now taken an additional step in scaling, started to incorporate some elements of a product business into your model, and have diversified your revenue stream.

Starting immediately on step 3 would be such a daunting task, that in the unlikely scenario she would succeed, it would still take her years to get anywhere with the project. Instead, she could be generating a sustainable income almost from day one!

This is just one real-world example. In fact, you might already have sufficient expertise and reputation to launch into a service business, or even a product business. Just make sure you aren't biting off more than you can chew. Don't be afraid to start small, dropping all the self-imposed obstacles of learn this and develop that, and just get started with whatever model is within (or close to) your current skill set!

Criteria #3 - Location versus Financial Independence

As I have mentioned before, different models offer varying levels of freedom. Working full-time as a freelancer for a particular company that requires a 40 hour work week, 9am-5pm during EST business hours, obviously is much less flexible than getting your income passively through publishing ebooks on Amazon, for example. Those two examples stand in stark contrast, but there are always many gray areas that are important to keep in mind, lest you end up with a business model that makes you miserable due to inflexibility.

For example, I spent about 6 months working 100% on digital product businesses, which are at the"most flexible"end of the spectrum. It didn't matter when I worked, if I took time off, or where I lived. Then I made the decision to launch a parallel service business (going in the opposite direction of most on the business model spectrum). All of the sudden I was accountable to a team I was training, and all my clients. Now there were no particular hours or location demanded of me, but I had still lost a substantial amount of freedom. I had clients that demanded my attention and team calls at odd hours due to timezone differences. I had to have a high level of email availability to cover emergencies for clients or for my team, so I lost the ability to take several days off whenever I wanted.

Boohoo Grant! Why don't you whine a bit more…

In reality I still had an insanely flexible job, but once you attain a certain level of freedom, and then lose it, trust me when I say that you feel it. This experience reminded me that, personally, I put a HUGE weight on freedom, flexibility, and independence, and I realized that I am willing to sacrifice significant income to maintain this flexibility. Despite the fact that the new business literally doubled my personal income and was fantastic in terms of branding and reputability, I quickly dissolved it after just 4 months - it was making me too unhappy!

Your preferences will vary. I know many people who actually enjoy some kind of structure, and don't do well with too much flexibility. To each their own, just be honest with yourself and choose what will make YOU happy. This will create the greatest level of sustainable success in the long-run.

Quick review of the 3 criteria to keep in mind when choosing your business model:

1.Timeline - pick a model consistent with the timeline of your goals and finances. Don't try to build a new Amazon if you only have a month.

2.Skill - pick a model that isn't too much of a stretch beyond your current knowledge and expertise. If you don't know the first thing about X, and aren't working with an industry expert, then don't start that business!

3.Freedom Preferences - if complete independence matters to you above all else, you might choose to push harder for a service or product model. But still don't forget criteria 1 and 2!

Of course you will want to weigh all three of these criteria for yourself. They are all interrelated, but might sometimes lead you in different directions. Then it just a matter of which criteria you weight the most for yourself!

One of the major goals of a service business is to free up your time and hire others who actually fulfill the service your business provides. After all, you've got more important things to do, like innovation in your business, connecting with cool people to create partnerships, and traveling!

One thing I found was hiring is considerably more complex than it sounds, even for simple positions, especially when you are doing it for the first time. Here are some best practices for hiring once you get to this stage (when you first start the business you will initially be doing most things yourself):

Leverage your network first:

Business is based on trust, between the company and the customer as well as within an organization. If trust fails you end up with unreliable or dishonest employees, or even worse: dissatisfied team members who do a poor job because maybe they don't trust you! Both sides of this are a challenge, and the easiest way to avoid these situations is to find someone who comes personally recommended from someone whose judgment you trust. Maybe it's a friend of a friend who's a great designer, or just some random VA they happened to find that they swear by - this is one of your best shots at quickly finding quality. But if that fails…

Cast your net far and wide, then screen ruthlessly (and automatically):

Spread the word for what you are looking for as wide as you can - job posting sites, forums, online communities, friends, wherever - then quickly and harshly weed out candidates as automatically as possible.

1.Include in the job description instructions to include a certain code word when people contact you or submit an application.

2.Cut anyone who can't follow that instruction.

3.Depending on the size of your pool, requiring applicants to create a youtube video or another customized task which is somewhat time consuming is also a great way to quickly screen people, merely based on whether they even do it or not.

4.Give out small sample projects to gauge their performance.

5.Cut those who don't follow those instructions well, deliver late, or any other red flags that go off in the back of your head.

6.Interview the last couple candidates - if they show up late that's a no-go - usually one or two will stand out. If you can't decide then do another sample project, making it a competition based on whatever criteria is most important (number of leads in 24 hours, how fast they can put together a proposal, etc.)

This approach ensures only the most motivated people into your organization.

Digital Product Businesses

You have knowledge and experience, why not turn it into an automated product so there is no service to perform and no team to manage? To be honest, I love digital products. They can be potentially a huge headache to get set up with initially, but once you pass that barrier, you have total freedom of time and location, because sales are global and largely automatic once you create the product and set up your funnels.

A quick note on price points…

When it comes to selling a product, especially a digital product, your margin will generally be much lower than a service business. Rather than taking home thousands per customer, you might only be netting $10 per customer, so to make a good income the scale is much larger (you service many more customers for the same income level). Many businesses have a variety of offerings which vary according to how educational vs customized they are. For example, one company I recently spoke with offers only educational materials at their lowest price point, a mix of education and services in the middle, and then 100% customized services at a much higher price point. You can even throw in a consulting option into that model as well.

Most Common Types of Digital Products

Online Courses

There are two primary options for providing online courses and getting paid to do it: using educational platforms or building your own community. The former is much easier to do, as you don't have to manage as many variables, including everything from website updates to (in some cases) marketing. That being said, the earning potential for building your own platform has amuchhigher earning potential, if you are willing to invest the time.

1.Educational Platforms (examples: Udemy, Skillfeed, Skillshare, etc.)

While there are dozens of educational platforms, my experiences (and greatest successes) have been through Udemy. In fact there is a good chance you found this book via my Udemy classes. For those who have expertise, and are looking at a faster return without having to deal with all the maintenance and marketing of building a website and community, Udemy is a fantastic option. If you make excellent content that people want to buy, and understand how to optimize for the platform, you basically just sit back and watch the money roll in. I spend only a couple hours a month on maintaining my courses on Udemy - the rest is spent improving them, building more, and working on other projects like this book!

People often ask what building a course looks like. If you are extremely efficient and build the right type of course, you can build it in as little as a week, and have it earning about a thousand dollars per month within the first month. The extreme examples would be making a course and launching the first version of it in as little as 48 hours (I've done this) or the courses that make $10,000+/month. These are the extremes of course, but the average person (who does everything right) can shoot for about a thousand per month. If you do the math, you could earn up to $4,000/month in the first 1-2 months alone, and there is a good chance this is more than you need!

2.Your Own Platform and Community

For those who choose this route, it is typically a longer and more arduous route, but with higher payouts for those who succeed. This is a business model that can generate millions per year (trust me, I've talked to people doing it) but usually takes several years of full-time work. And even once you get to that point, it is nowhere near passive income. In many ways it is a dramatically different working situation than the education platform option, so choose which is more aligned with your goals: faster payout of passive income but relatively low earning ceiling versus massive earning potential but a long payout that requires continuous work.

Ebooks

Similar to online courses, your two main options are publishing platforms (mostly Amazon Kindle) and your own platform (discussed next under Product Launches).

In many ways publishing an ebook for Kindle is extremely similar to publishing an online course for Udemy - it takes as little as a week, is super scalable, and is almost completely passive income once it's done. One difference is that ebooks tend to make more like $200-400/month, however the earnings are more consistent and durable than Udemy. I'm sure you already see how quickly you add up some $1,000/month courses and $200-400/month ebooks to build a digital product empire!

There are some tricks to launching Kindle books of course, but to be honest it really isn't that complicated! In fact plenty of entrepreneurs even create Kindle publishing businesses rather than writing themselves - they build a team of people that find topics, write the book, then goes through the launch. Product Launches

Somewhere in the middle between using a content publishing platform and building your own is a product launch. This usually refers to a bundle of an ebook alongside a short video course on a particular topic, and is primarily sold through email lists. In fact you can create a product and get it quickly in front of tens of thousands of peoplewithoutyour own email list by leveraging affiliate partners. There are armies of people looking around on Clickbank and similar platforms for great products to promote, although it's very much recommended to establish relationships with affiliate partners rather than relying on such platforms.

A friend of mine recently got started with product launches and quickly made over $150,000 in about 9 months starting with no email list. Oh and did I mention he was 23 at the time?

Physical Product Businesses

Note: this will be a relatively short section, as I have not created a physical product business myself, and only write about what I have direct experience with. The information in this section comes from interviewing successful entrepreneurs with these types of business.

The story lines of people who end up running physical product businesses differ slightly than those who create digital products or who run service businesses. With this type of business, specific types of previous experience does not always directly lead to this model. For example, someone might be a freelance marketing consultant, leading to a marketing agency, then they also start some physical product businesses. Did their previous experience lead to it? Surely they are better at marketing that product, but not directly. These types of businesses are at the more challenging end of the spectrum, but have less concrete prerequisites to start.

For those of you just getting started with this model, I've put together some of the easiest methodologies to use to get started. Give these a go before you try to start the next Walmart.

Dropshipping

A popular method of selling physical products while holding no inventory, you basically serve as a sales channel for companies that create specific types of physical products, then they fulfill the orders, while your company is the customer-facing brand.

You can seethis interview(youtu.be/DMOOts5sIXY)that I did with Johnny FD who talks about how he makes his living this way.

Simple Travel Businesses

I have two friends who have started physical product businesses while traveling, both of which essentially relate back to providing a product that is cheaply supplied in one part of the world to another location that more highly values it - a business which brings that new/different/cheaper/etc. product to a new market.

The simplest version of this model is what one friend of mine does - she finds products while traveling that she likes that she knows people in her network would be interested in having, but can't easily get. She then figures out her margins once factoring in shipping so that she can make about a 50% profit and puts the word out across social media and a couple other platforms. Sound too simple? She tells me she works about an hour a day and it more than pays for her travels. Two recent examples were locally produced honey and feather pillows that she found in Thailand.

Another friend of mine sells commodities like salt and sugar - literally thousands of pounds of it - to restaurants and businesses back home. As a world traveler, she knows where to get it cheaper than they do and so she makes a profit by providing that service to them.

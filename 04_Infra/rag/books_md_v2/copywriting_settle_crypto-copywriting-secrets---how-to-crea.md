## Chapter 1 -

## Everything You Ever Wanted to Know About Writing Headlines... But Were Afraid to Ask


Dear Crypto Marketer,


Today's newsletter is the first in a series of 5 issues showing you, step by step, how to write profitable sales letters.


We'll primarily focus on sales letters selling info-products (i.e. books, DVD's, CD's, etc) as that's what the majority of subscribers sell. However, you can still apply all of it to selling anything else, too. For example, I use this same "formula" when writing ads selling videos just as easily as if I'm writing an ad selling a supplement. And the "secret formula" goes simply like this:


- Headline


- Lead


- Story


- Bullets


- Close


Nothing sexy or "world class" about it, is there? But methinks you'll find that my simple process of creating each of these components makes hammering out one profitable ad after another much, much easier - and maybe even "routine."


Sound cool?


Okay then, let's start with headlines. The first thing about writing headlines is... forget this whole idea of creating "killer" headlines. In fact, avoid writing headlines other copywriters and marketers consider "killer" or "cool" or any nonsense like that. What we want are headlines that get your sales letter read, not that get all the copywriting fanboys raving in the Internet marketing forums about how "killer" it is. And the first step of writing headlines that get your ad read is to make sure you are 1000% totally in "sync" with what your market wants (and doesn't want). So to that end, here's a list of questions to ask about your market before putting pen to paper:


- What keeps them AWAKE at night?


- What are they AFRAID of?


- What causes them PAIN?


- What HUMILIATES them?


- What makes them ANGRY?


- Who are they FURIOUS with?


- What do they WORRY about most?


- What are their VALUES like?


- What's their most URGENT, "hot button" crisis right now?


- Who are their ENEMIES?


- What INCOME bracket are they in?


- What are their POLITICAL views?


- What FRUSTRATES them most?


- What do they WANT more than anything?


- What RELIGION (if any) are they?


These questions get you thinking about your prospects and their problems, instead of your product and its features. And it goes back to the old adage:


"Don't tell me about your weed killer, tell me about my crab grass!"


In other words... talk about their problems, desires or wants, and you enter their "world." Once inside, you'll never bore them, lose their interest, or be ignored.


Okay, so that's step numero uno - get in sync with your market. Step two is the "what" to say. Check this out: Once upon a time, one of the "old school" advertising agencies (I forget which one) invented what they called "knock-knocks" to write their headlines. And it works like this: Imagine you are selling your product (whatever it is) door to door. No email, Internet or technology. And let's say you find a neighborhood where a lot of your prospects who have the problem your product solves live. So you walk up to one of the houses and you knock on the door. As soon as that door opens, what can you say, in one sentence, to the person who opens the door that:


1. Makes it impossible for them to slam the door on your face (like they might normally do to a sales rep)... and...


2. Gets them to literally invite you inside and beg you to tell them more?


In other words... what's the one thing you can say, to that prospect, at that exact moment in their life, with their unique problems, desires, fears and wants, that would make them practically jump for joy you showed up on their doorstep? For example, maybe you're writing to a bunch of guys who are getting older, are sort of "type A" weekend warriors who play hard, and have fantasies about being tough guys, kicking butt, and maybe even have regrets they didn't join the military or are starting to miss being in the military, etc. And because they are getting up there in age, they're starting to feel aches and pains, starting to worry about things like heart disease, cholesterol and "brain fog." They're also realizing they aren't the young, slim, "indestructible" men they were 10 years ago. In that case, you may use something like:


World's Leading Military Combat Instructor "De-classifies" The Secret Supplement Used By The Navy SEALS To Battle Pain, Injury, Body Fat And Cardiovascular Health Problems


See how that works?


Okay then, now that we know the "what" to say, it's time to learn "how" to say it. So what we're going to do now is, we're going to cover 10 different "fool proof" ways to write headlines. These are not the only ways to do it. But they are easy to use, and are surprisingly effective. Here goes...


1. The Proof Headline: Thank God for Gary Bencivenga. Before he started teaching his secrets publicly in his ezine (http://www.marketingbullets.com), most of us whipper-snapper copywriters had no idea how powerful using proof is. Take for example, the "Navy Seal" headline above. If it simply said, "How to Combat Pain, Injury, Body Fat and Cardiovascular Health Problems" it wouldn't have near the impact it does by adding in the part about the client being the world's #1 military close combat instructor, and how the Navy Seals use his supplement. Like Gary B. says, join proof to your promise and response almost always goes up.


2. Market Oriented Headline: You hardly ever see a goo-roo use or even talk about these. But I can tell you this, headlines that address the market (and don't even necessarily make a claim) can explode your response. Example? Okay, here's a headline I wrote over a year and a half ago that's still going strong (even on high traffic news sites, like WorldNetDaily, Drudge, etc.): Warning: Do Not Read This If You Have Moral, Ethical or Religious Reasons Against Hurting (or Even Killing) Someone Who Violently Attacks You, Your Wife or Your Kids. No claim, benefit or anything "killer" about it, is there? It simply addresses the market.


3. The Emotion Headline: This is where you tap into a strong emotion your prospect has. A "real life" example is this bad-boy that ran for over a year straight: "Warning: Do Not Read This Unless You Are Frustrated as Hell with Your Golf Swing." Notice that "Warning" word again? And lookie - no claim or benefit!


4. The Offer Headline: This is pretty straight forward. If you are writing to people who already want what you have, this can work like gangbusters. Example: "Free Fuel for a Year with Purchase of New Car - Today Only"


5. The Contrast Headline: I love using contrast in my writing. Especially contrasting ideas. They immediately grab attention and they are very hard to ignore when properly targeted. Examples: "Beat the Races by Picking Losers!" or "How to Lose Weight by Eating More of These Fatty Foods"


6. The Tabloid Headline: There's a reason tabloids get such high readership and sales: The headlines! Just add a sensational "twist" to your claims. Like, for example, John Carlton's now-famous "one legged golfer" ad: "Amazing Secret Discovered by One-Legged Golfer Adds 50 Yards to Your Drives, Eliminates Hooks and Slices… And Can Slash Up to 10 Strokes from Your Game Almost Overnight!"


7. The Curiosity Headline: I didn't realize just how powerful curiosity was until master copywriter Scott Haines (whose clients have included Jay Abraham and Donald Trump) said that was the #1 most powerful reason people buy. Even more powerful than what people want! Here are some examples from legendary copywriter Mel Martin: "What Never to Eat On an Airplane" and "Bills It's Okay to Pay Late"


8. The Straight Benefit Headline: Hey, sometimes you can just say what you have to say with no messing around. Especially when talking about a hot problem people are really fired up about. Here's a classic Eugene Schwartz headline. Think this may get read if gas prices soar? "Get Up to 12 Miles more Per Gallon from Your Car"


9. The Question Headline: I once heard it's neurologically impossible for someone to ignore a question that's of interest to him or her. Is that really true? I don't know. But I do know question headlines work exceptionally well. Plus, there's no hypey feel to them and they don't look like "sales pitches." Here's a great example from copywriter Brian Keith Voiles: "Which of These Health Problems Do You Want To Beat?"


10. The "Combine & Conquer" Headline: For best results, try to combine variations of the above. For example, combining a proof headline with a question headline ("When Doctors Have Headaches, What Do They Do?") or an emotion headline with a market-oriented headline ("An Open Letter to Readers of Black Belt Magazine Who Are Fed Up with the Hype Surrounding the Seemingly Endless String of 'Fighter-Of-The-Month' Programs"), and so on and so forth.


These 10 formats are some of the best you can use. Just remember, you're always safe in your prospect's world. Write your headlines inside their world and you can never go wrong (no matter how you "dress" it up).


## Questions from The Crypto Marketing Mail Bag


QUESTION: Ben, any tips on setting up joint ventures? I have a product I want to sell to some big name lists but they don't even bother answering my emails!


Great question! Mayhaps these tips will help get your foot in a door or two...


- Sell to buyers: Sales 101, my friend. Seek people already doing a lot of JV's and affiliate offers in your market. They will be far more likely to hear you out and/or JV with you. Frankly, they may even be waiting for you!


- Send a question, not your product: Never send your product to people unsolicited. I don't care how "killer" it is, either. Instead, ask them a question, respect their time and remove all pressure: "Do you do JV's with people who sell ____? I realize you have to be careful who you associate with, so I completely understand if the answer is no. But I have a product that can help your list____. If you're at all interested, I would be happy to send it to you. There's no pressure, either way. And if you decide to pass on selling it, that's perfectly okay..."


- Don't be afraid to start small: You can also do reciprocal JV's with people of similar sized lists as yours. So if your list is 200 people, cross promote with someone else with a 200-person list (they'll be more likely to JV than someone with a list of, say, 20,000). If you're hell bent on JV'ing with a goo-roo, then try this: If you have 300 people on your list ask if they'd do an offer to 300 people on their list and you'll send an offer to your 300. This would also give you some potential social proof action for later (see below).


- Social proof: As you do more JV's, start using that "social proof" to your advantage. It's much easier to JV with Goo-roo Bob if you already JV'd with his best friends Goo-roo Joe or Newbie Nick (and vice versa). You're more "safe" to do business with when you have that connection.

## Chapter 2 -

## How to Nab More Smackeroos with a "Can't Ignore" Sales Letter Lead


Dear Crypto Marketer,


No time for dilly-dally today.


We're gonna mush on with our "how to write a sales letter" lesson. Last month, we talked about a good sales letter "skeleton" (headline-lead-story-bullets-close), and we covered 10 simple ways to write headlines. This time we're going to talk about the sales letter "lead." The lead rarely gets much attention for some reason. Yet, it is almost as important as the almighty headline itself! After all, once your headline gets their attention, you have to say something to keep that attention locked in for the rest of the ride. And if that opening ain't packed with the right words and emotion... you're dead in the water, amigo.


So on that note... below are several "user friendly" ways to write a lead.


They are not the only ways to do it. And, in some cases, they may not even be the best ways. But they work like gangbusters, and are pretty much the only ones I use with my own ads these days.


Okay, enough build up, let's roll...


1. The "Candor" Lead: I first saw this one in a Gary Halbert ad and have successfully used it twice since (and will probably use it again soon). As a side note: Gary Halbert is by far my biggest influence when it comes to sales letter leads. So most of these will be leads he used a lot and/or invented. Anyway, where were we? Oh yeah, the candor lead. Look, candor (i.e. being forthright about flaws and/or limitations) is huge. Especially in this age of over-hyped promises. And that's why this ad is so effective in competitive markets. The best example I've seen of this is Gary Halbert's ad for "Betty's Diet Book":


My name is Betty Adams and, the first thing you should know about me is... I am not a doctor. I'm not an expert on nutrition either. I never went to college and I don't have a degree in anything. In fact, the only thing I consider myself an 'expert' about is...


Here's one way I've adapted it:


My name is Steve Habib and, the first thing you should know about me is... I am not a veterinarian. I'm not an expert in dog medicine either. I never went to college and I don't have a degree in anything even remotely related to dogs. In fact, the only thing I consider myself an 'expert' about is...


See how that works?


Oddly enough, sometimes telling people you're not an authority makes everything else you say more believable.


2. The "Did You Know" Lead: This is great for wrenching on peoples' sense of curiosity. Remember last month when I cited world class copywriter Scott Haines (whose clients have included everyone from Jay Abraham to Donald Trump)? And how he was saying curiosity is the #1 reason people buy? Well, one of the beauties of this opening is it's pure curiosity, and practically impossible for targeted people to ignore. Here's an example from Gary Bencivenga's awesome "Interviews That Win Jobs" ad:


NEW YORK-Do you know why you should never eat a spinach salad when you're being interviewed over lunch? If you're a man, do you know why you should never wear a gold bracelet to a job interview? If you're a woman, do you know the kind of blouse that makes interviewers of both sexes think you're a loser?


Here's a crude (but successful) attempt at the same opening:


Did you know you should never give your dog a bone? Or let him chew on a tennis ball? Or allow him to so much as walk on your driveway? It's true. In fact, any one of these "innocent" little activities...


3. The "If/Then" Lead: This is my favorite opening, is the easiest to use and (as Eugene Schwartz says in his masterpiece book Breakthrough Advertising) has "built in belief" within the structure. Here are some examples:


If you would like to be a woman that nearly all men... just can't resist, this is going to be the most important message you ever read.


If you would like to 'download' the world's deadliest self-defense moves directly into your brain... just by turning on your TV and without even having to get off your couch... then this letter will show you how.


If you would like to sponsor as many as 25 (or more) new reps per month... and do it even if you're shy, introverted and scared to death of talking to prospects on the phone now... then this letter will show you how.


4. The "If/Then" Lead (with extra proof): This is just like the "if/then" lead above... only with an extra twist of proof and credibility (or curiosity) to it. Here's the original version (as far as I can tell, at least) of this opening from Thomas Hall:


If you would like to lose up to 63 pounds, then here is how I did it in Japan and why I think you can do it in America.


And here are a couple ways I've adapted it:


If you want to know how even a 'puny' man can control and dominate violent criminals (even a mob of violent criminals) barehanded... then here's how some of the world's most elite law enforcement agents do it. And why you can do the same thing with the information in this letter.


If you would like to know a secret way to make yourself near-invincible in a fight... then here's how ancient gladiators, Samurai and other deadly warriors in history did it. And why you can do the same thing today with the information in this letter.


That extra pinch of "spice" (i.e. "here's how so-and-so did it...") makes quite an impact, doesn't it?


5. The "Question" Lead: This one is pretty simple - just ask a question that is relevant to your market's pains and/or desires. It's just like last months' question headline, where it's almost neurologically impossible for someone to ignore a question that is about them and their pains, frustrations, desires, etc. Examples:


If money were no object, would you own Encyclopedia Britannica?


Do you have hemorrhoids?


Did you know that the long term performance of baseball cards beats the pants off of stock, T-bills, and rare coins?


6. The "Bite Sized" Lead: These are really short openings, just a few words. (Or even one word!) I admit, I have not used this very much. But it's brilliant in its simplicity and long on impact (if targeted towards your market's desires, fears or emotional state). Plus, in some ways, it's easier to read than not to read. Here are a few examples:


I was the total skeptic.


Picture this. (Note: this leads perfectly into a story... which we'll yap about more in next month's issue)


Recently I received a bizarre phone call.


7. The "Would You Take Advice from...?" Lead: I've only seen this lead one other time. (In a Gary Halbert ad for a series of reports called "The Crime Connection.") It's tops for selling a product that can be positioned as something that's ethical, but that someone "shady" is using. Here's Gary's example:


Would you take investment advice from a drug dealer? Sounds like a silly question, doesn't it? Well, don't make up your mind too quickly - I've got a true story to tell you that sounds like something right out of The Godfather.


Whoa! How can you not keep reading that bad-boy? Here's an example of how I recently used this opening:


Would you take fighting advice from a bloated, out of shape and overweight criminal scum bag? Sounds like a silly question, doesn't it? Well, don't make up your mind too quickly. Because I've got a story to tell you that sounds like something right out of a Hollywood "gangsta" flick!


8. The "Please Take a Moment" Lead: Another version I have not used. But if I was selling a product that had a "before and after" component (especially a dramatic photo), I would use this in a heartbeat. There's no way to explain this one, though. You have to see it with these two Gary Halbert masterpieces:


If you have a weight problem, I want you to take a good look at the pictures on this page. (Note: there is an extremely dramatic "before and after" photo next to it)


Please take a moment to look at the photograph I am sending you with this letter. (Note: There is a photo that went with this letter of a newspaper headline during the start of the great depression).


9. A Couple More Examples: Below are a couple more excellent leads for you to adapt, tweak or be inspired by -


When you were young, a health nut was someone who sprinkled wheat germ on his cornflakes. In those days, your choice of medical care was pretty limited: You could pick Dr. Smith or Dr. Jones. And neither of them suspected that vitamin C was good for anything but curing scurvy. (A classic Jim Rutz opening)


The "experts" say that individual investors like you and me are motivated by only two emotions - fear and greed. (From Gary Bencivenga's brilliant "Lies, Lies, Lies" package)


Anyway, the sales letter lead/opening paragraph is often the hardest part of an ad for most people to write. (Even tougher than the headline.)


I hope the above takes the "sting" out of it for you.

### Questions from The Crypto Marketing Mail Bag


QUESTION: I was listening to Clayton Makepeace speak about editing. He said he would basically brain dump all his ideas onto the page and then edit from there (I get that.)


He would make "editing passes" on his copy like: (I'm just guessing here) "Okay, this pass I'm going to check all the proof elements" "Next, I'll check my verbs and adjectives. Are they punchy enough?" "Next, I'll check my credibility elements." and so on. So my question is, do you have a systematic way you edit copy? Do you make specific, ordered, "editing passes"? If so, what are some of the major editing areas you concentrate on and in what order?


Since we're talking about sales letters for the next few months, this is a nice, timely question. To answer the question... yes, I do have a systematic way of editing sales copy. However, it's not nearly as "sexy" as Clayton Makepeace's method. And, in fact, is probably a lot more grueling.


But it works way better than any other I've used.


Here it is...


- Get to the point where it's readable. In other words, you would never show it to anyone, but everything is in order and the basic ideas are all there.


- Read 9 times - out loud. Yes, this will drive your spouse and (in my case, at least) your dog bonkers. Might as well expect it and tell your loved ones to make the appropriate plans to deal with it.


- Fix stuff as you go through each pass. The first 2 or 3 read-throughs will be slow... and maybe even kind of painful. But you'll catch dozens of things you would miss otherwise. By the 6th or 7th reading (especially if it's a long letter) you'll start crying for your mama from the tedious nature of it. Too bad. This must be done 9 times, so just keep at it. Try to break it up into several days. And trust me on this, you will get ideas with each read through that will make your sales letter much stronger. Plus, you'll be tightening it to the point where there will not be one sentence, word or punctuation mark that does not earn its keep.


- Go back over other competing ads and research. After your 9th reading, go through your sales letter again (silently, not out loud). But this time, do it with your research notes, facts and the best competing ads you can find. You want to make sure you do not miss any benefit that can be included, or any fact, or any other dramatic "twist" to make your ad all it can be.


- Send to your mastermind. You do belong to a mastermind group (or two or three), don't you? If not, join or start one. This is important because, at this point, you're way to close to everything. They will pick out errors, "cracks" in your arguments, and bring up objections you may have missed. Just be careful, though. You are seeking help with the "mechanics" here. If someone is not actually in the market, and they say something that doesn't jibe with what you know is true of your market, you can safely ignore the advice. But it's always good to get this kind of feedback - even if the criticism stings. (Hey, nothing puts hair on your chest like stinging criticism of writing you've slaved over for weeks on end.) And by the way, if possible, let some people in your market read it, too. Their reaction is worth solid gold. If they say, "how can I get this?" then you've got a winner. If not, ask what it would take for them to buy it and adjust your ad (and product) accordingly.


- Cool off. Take some time to chill out and not read it for as long as you can. The longer the better. (I usually take at least a weekend).


- 10th reading. Now it's time to read it one final time. Usually you will not see any problems. But it's good to do it just in case. Especially if you added/subtracted stuff due to your mastermind group and/or market feedback.


- Run it up the flagpole. Now it's time to send it to your client or run that puppy (if you are your own client) up the flagpole (i.e., test it) and see if it "salutes."


Tedious?


Definitely. But it's simple and mechanical. And it works like crazy to keep your copy 100% clear.

## Chapter 3 -

## 6 Ways to Write Hot Sales Letter Stories That Bring In Lots of the Green Stuff (Even If You Screw Everything Else Up!)


Dear Crypto Marketer,


Ready to continue our "how to write a sales letter" lesson? So far, we've covered the first two bones in the sales letter "skeleton": Headlines and leads. Today it's all about story, baby. I sometimes take flack for saying it - but I believe a good story aimed at the right market can carry the sale all on its lonesome. In other words, even if the rest of your ad is kind of weak (lame bullets, bland guarantee, no offer, zero call to action, etc.), a good story can still maketh you mucho of the green stuff.


Prove it, you say?


Okay, wise guy, I will. Check out these apples:


Back in 1986 a movie called Top Gun hit the theaters. Top Gun is about a couple hotshot Naval pilots given a chance to train with the "best of the best" pilots in the world at the "Top Gun" fighter pilot school. Now, whether you like Top Gun or not (I love it!), it was, in some ways, an extremely profitable "sales letter", for two reasons:


1. After the movie hit the screens, Ray-Ban Aviator sunglasses (the kind Tom Cruise's character "Maverick" wore) jumped 40%. That's a huge increase.


2. Air Force and Navy recruitment (both sometimes very "hard sells") shot through the roof. In fact, the movie was so good at "selling" all the young whippersnappers of the day, they had recruitment booths in many of the theaters while the movie was playing!


And it was all in a big part due to the story.


After all, there was nothing in the movie telling you to go buy Maverick's brand of sunglasses or to join the armed forces. Yet, the movie "sold" great hordes of people on wanting to be like Maverick. To live that exact lifestyle, get the hot chick, and have some friggin' adventure in their lives.


Such is the power of stories.


Which is why, in my humble (but accurate) opinion, the right story, to the right market, can do more to carry the sale than any fancy copywriting tricks or tips. And today we're going to cover six ways to write profitable sales letter stories (each of which have been proven to work in real ads - no theory allowed). Just take one of the formats below, plug it into your ad, and let the profits flow.


So are you ready, Maverick? Okay then, let's get. it. on:


Sales Storytelling Format #1: Personal Story from Your Perspective


This is probably the most common sales letter story. And what you do is, you basically "walk" people (step-by-step) through a painful problem you went through to get the result your readers are looking for.


For example, if you sell a report on how to get rid of painful urinary tract infections, you would tell the story about all the pain your urinary tract infection caused, what it was like, how nothing you tried got rid of it, the embarrassment, humiliation and all the other physical and psychological horrors you felt (that you know your market is feeling, too). Then, one day, you figured out a way to get rid of that infection without drugs or expensive medicine, and everything was hunky dory from there... etc. So it's like a "quest" where you set out to get the answers to some problem you have and how you overcame it.


Here are a couple examples for study:


- http://www.SwipeFileSecrets.com/images/Desperate-Wife.html


- http://www.SwipeFileSecrets.com/nerd.pdf


Sales Storytelling Format #2: Story About Someone Else (Non-Fiction)


This is a story where, instead of telling your own story, you tell the real life story of someone else who had the same problem your market does. So, for example, let's say you sell a video about how to make money on the Internet if you're just starting out. You can tell the story (assuming it's true, of course) about someone in a bad economy who was out of work, had lots of bills to pay and couldn't find a job if her life depended on it. Her kids needed to be taken care of (food, clothing, medicine, etc.), her unemployment was running out and no matter what she tried, everything failed. But one day she was surfing the Internet and discovered a way anyone can use even if you have zero Internet experience, etc.


Again, walk the reader through the frustrations and problems they are already facing, through the actions of someone else (besides you) to get in "sync" with them, and then lead into the product.


Here are a couple prime examples:


- http://www.BenSettle.com/Swipe-File/cripple-golf.pdf


- http://www.BenSettle.com/Swipe-File/Combat-JKD


Sales Storytelling Format #3: Story about Someone Else (Dramatized)


This is where you tell the story about a fictional person your market can identify with in some way, and tell that person's story about how they achieved the same goal your market wants for themselves (to solve a painful problem or achieve some desire, and so on).


A word of Warning: You should not say a fictional person used your product. One famous example of this is John Caples' classic "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano" ad (You can see it at: http://www.BenSettle.com/Swipe-File/Caples-Piano.pdf). No offense to the late Mr. Caples, but it's misleading, IMHO. Why?


Because it talks about a fictional person who learned how to play the piano using the course being sold in the ad - even though the person never existed (as far as we know). This is like me telling a story about a fictional copywriter making a million dollars writing an ad and then saying he did it using the info in my Copywriting Grab Bag book. When, in reality, he did not use my book because he never existed.


However, parables are a different matter. This is where you use a story as an example of what is possible, for inspiration or to show the importance of why someone would want to have what you're selling - but with no mention of the product made. Here are a couple examples:


- http://www.LawDogTactics.com


- http://www.BulletsBatsBlades.com


Sales Storytelling Format #4: Historical Story


This format is probably my favorite. It's extremely persuasive, contains zero hint of anything even remotely resembling "hype" and can persuade people to buy things maybe they wouldn't have otherwise. And all you do is talk about something that happened in history and relate it to what you're selling (the only "drawback" with this is you obviously have to do your homework to find the right historical example).


For example: I once had to write an ad selling a grappling/wrestling DVD course to people who usually hate the thought of having to sweat or roll around on a matt, etc, (they wanted the "push button ninja" solution to self-defense). So I did some research online (maybe 30 minutes, nothing big) and discovered certain people used grappling and wrestling in the ancient Roman coliseums to fight lions (barehanded). And I also read how the ancient Samurai used to terrorize westerners with their "bizarre" way of fighting (i.e. grappling). Suddenly, grappling went from something that seemed dirty and sweaty and hard... to exciting and even fun. Here are some examples of how to use historical stories (including the grappling story):


- http://www.GuerrillaGrappling.com


- http://www.UltimateColonCleanse.com


- http://www.CloseCombatTraining.com


Sales Storytelling Format #5: Meet The Guru Story


This one is related to the personal story, but it's got a bit more "pop" to it due to the built-in credibility it gives you. With this format, you tell a personal story about how you met/talked with a guru who showed you how to overcome your problems or achieve what you were looking to achieve. It can be as simple as the time you spent learning from them on the phone... to as dramatic as traveling up the forbidden mountain to learn at their feet.


Pretty simple stuff, really. Here are some examples:


- http://www.BlackbeltRecruiting.com/letter.php


- http://www.KensCopyClinic.com


- http://www.ScientificSelfDefense.com


Sales Storytelling Format #6: "Mix And Match" Stories


Finally, you can always "mix and match" the various formats.


For example, you can combine telling your own story with a story about someone else. Or the conversation with the guru can be mixed with a historical story. There are no "rules" to this other than (1) never be boring and (2) always make sure the story is something your market will be interested in. The links below are some examples of mix & matched story formats. See if you can't figure out which story formats are embedded within:


- http://www.CryptoMarketing.com/stuff/golf.pdf


- http://www.EscapeCombatives.com


Anyway, so there you have it:


Six sales storytelling formats that are proven to work. And by the way, these story formats are great for ANY kind of selling. I don't care if you're selling in print (i.e., sales letter), using podcasts, videos, articles, blogs, talking on the phone, selling in person, etc. Stories are the chief means by which humans have communicated for thousands of years. We're "hard wired" for stories. And even in memory training seminars they tell you to put complex facts and things you want to remember in story format for easier remembrance.

If something seems "off" - such as if a different ad is up on one of those links without a story, for example, or if the link is dead - I apologize in advance.

### Critique Time


Speaking of opt-in pages...


This month's critique is an opt-in (i.e. "squeeze") page.


Here's the scoop: My friend across the pond Katie Williams is a nutritionist and wanted to know how she could tweak her opt-in page for more response. Below is her ad, followed by some ideas she can use (and you can use, even if you're not in the same niche as her) for a stronger opt-in pitch:


Feel Great Now!


Just leave your details below to receive my special 8-page report "Feel Great Now."


In this report, written by Katie Williams, nutritionist, you'll learn:


- Why calling the doctor might be a huge mistake!


- Why we are becoming a nation of sissies and hypochondriacs, and how to stop this happening to you!


- Why most people have no idea what being well and healthy actually means!


- Why trusting your doctor might have fatal consequences!


- The truth, once and for all, about dieting!


And much more…


Important:


When you submit your details below, you'll be sent an email asking you to confirm your request. Please check your inbox and junk folder for this email, and click the link to confirm your request for this special report. And read the report straight away, as it contains a very exclusive offer that will expire in 48 hours' time.


Okay, Katie, here are some suggestions-


1. Add credential to headline: A headline without a solid credential is like Batman without Robin. Or The Lone Ranger without Tonto. Or The Green Hornet without Kato. Or... you get the picture!


Seriously though, the headline I'm about to suggest may make you flinch at first. But if you at least test it, I think you'll find it makes a big difference (both on this capture page and any other sales letters in the same market). And here it is:


Free Report by England's Leading Authority on Health and Nutrition Reveals How to "Reset" Your Body Back to Optimal Health


Okay, two things: First, the "reset" part is just what I could come up with. It's not great, but just wanted to get you thinking in terms of making your claims dramatic and unique (more on that below). And secondly, what's really important here is the credential. Since I don't live in Jolly Old England, I don't know if anyone has claimed the title "England's Leading Authority On Health and Nutrition" or not. But if not, there is absolutely no reason why Katie Williams can't claim it. And, once you claim it, put it in your headlines, business cards, press releases (especially your press releases), etc. Also, it does not matter if you are the most educated or experienced or the "best" nutritionist, either. The loot goes to the person who claims the title of "best in the world." Whether they really are or not is always subjective (i.e. Dr. Phil, Bob Villa, Cesar Millan, etc. - none are the "best" at what they do, but they claimed the title - or let others give it to them.)


2. Spice it up: It's always good to make things that sound like anyone could make the same claim (i.e. "feel great") more dramatic - to set you apart from the crowd. And one way to do that is to add a "twist" to the words or use words that are unpredictable. You can also use terminology people associate with something they use every day. So, for example, "reset your health" might immediately make one think of rebooting their computer or resetting a clock to the right time, etc. Whatever the case, you may want to put some thought into that when crafting the headline and the bullets (we'll get into the bullets below).


3. Tell your story: Keeping with this issue's main them, "storytelling", this would be a perfect chance for you to take any of the formats discussed and use them on your capture page. You don't have to tell a super long story (although they can work fine, too, on capture pages). But just something like, "My name is Katie Williams. I'm a qualified nutritionist and one of the leading members of the Complementary Medical Association (CMA). I'm the author of x number of books on health and nutrition. And I've also given talks to prestigious companies about nutrition, such as Xerox..." (Incidentally, you can easily take all those ezine or blog articles you've been writing and turn them into books and - presto - instant author! Also, I think I remember you saying you've given talks at some big companies? Like Xerox? I can't remember, but whatever they were, definitely add them in, along with any other "credibility enhancers" you have.)


4. More bullets: This is something I preach from the rooftops. It's also (ironically) what the entire next Crypto Marketing Newsletter issue will be about. And that is bullets. You can never have too many bullets - as long as they are interesting to the reader. It says your report is 8 pages. If possible, maybe beef it up with 30 or 40 quick tips (that are not well known, yet fascinating to people who hear them for the first time). Then, go through it and write lots and lots of bullets based on those tips. Here's a chapter from The Copywriting Grab Bag that can help you crank out some decent bullets:


http://www.CopywritingGrabBag.com/bull.html


5. Write the bullets first: And finally, for the grand finale... another way to rev up those opt-ins is this:


- Write out all the benefits you know your market wants - no matter how impossible they may seem


- Write bullets for each of those "super benefits"


- Create a new report that delivers on as many of those benefits as possible


Believe it or not, this is how many of the world's most successful direct mail copywriters do it. They imagine they've been granted unlimited "genie like" powers to create the perfect product (or, in this case, the perfect opt-in bonus). Then, they write their advertising around that perfect product. After that, they try to create a product that fulfills as many of those benefits as possible (so they essentially write the ad before the product - and it works like crazy).

## Chapter 4 -

## 12 Ways to Write Bullets Points That Give Prospects Almost NO CHOICE but to Buy from Your Sales Letters


Dear Crypto Marketer,


Scooting right along with our "how to write a sales letter" lesson - today we're gonna talk about writing bullets. Lots and lots and lots of bullets. Because, in the end, it's all about the bullets, baby.


In fact, as Ken McCarthy says in his magnificent copywriting course (http://www.KensCopyClinic.com): Bullet writing is the one "skill" you must have to create truly effective ads. For one thing, bullets are easy on the eyes and quick for your brain to "digest." (There's a reason we write grocery lists and not grocery paragraphs when shopping). And some of the most successful ads in history are nothing but a headline, an opening paragraph... and bullets. Frankly, one good bullet "shot" inside your prospect's psychology can drive them to the point of madness if they don't buy. This is why (for example) I average well over 50 bullets in most of my ads. (My Copywriting Grab Bag ad has almost 200 bullets!)


Plus, here's another thing to consider:


Bullets are the raw material for everything else in your ads, too. When you know how to write bullets, you can write awesome P.S.'s offers, guarantees, subheads and, yes, headlines. Again, as Ken McCarthy teaches, "what is your headline but your best bullet?" So it's all about the bullets. Master this skill and you become a master copywriter. And today I'm going to show you how to hammer out dozens (even hundreds) of bullets for your products that drive your readers crazy with desire, curiosity and the "urge" to buy.


So without further ado...


Bullet Point Secret #1: Curiosity


This bullet is pretty straight forward. You simply say something that wrenches on your prospect's natural sense of curiosity. It goes back to what master copywriter Scott Haines told me he learned from the late (great) Sir Gary of Halbert: Curiosity is an even more powerful buying motive than desire:


- 17 lies all intelligent women always tell men!


- Cancelled check for fire insurance premium proves you're covered. Right? Wrong.


Bullet Point Secret #2: "Can't Be Done"


This kind of bullet is related to the curiosity bullet above. And I first heard of it in a lecture by copywriting legend Eugene Schwartz. These are bullets where the benefit or claim seems almost unbelievable (but still possible):


- A secret, "back door" way of getting Google to reduce your pay-per-click fees... without even logging into your AdWords account.


- How to vacuum, dust and clean your dresser drawer without removing its contents!


Bullet Point Secret #3: Proof & Credibility


Whenever you add credibility (or "proof") to a claim, you exponentially increase its believability. And since (as the "greatest living copywriter" Gary Bencivenga says) nobody buys without belief, adding credibility to your bullets will often increase your overall response, too:


- A scientifically proven way (discovered by Isaac Newton) to hit another man with "sledge hammer" like force. (Regardless of your size or speed now.)


- The one word Socrates (perhaps the greatest master of persuasion who ever lived) used to "sell" people on his ideas - even though they were dead set against him before!


Bullet Point Secret #4: Contrast


Contrasting ideas, words and concepts get big attention in advertising. That's why you see headlines about one-legged golfers and other sensationalist ideas in high-pulling ads. They also make great bullets, too:


- The incredible copywriting secret (used by both P.T. Barnum and Mother Theresa) that lets you sell to even your coldest, most "sales resistant" prospects.


- A sneaky way to sell more products with a weak sales pitch


Bullet Point Secret #5: Pain (Physical Or Psychological)


Memory training experts will tell you to painfully "peg" things to your body (in your mind) to remember something. Example, if you want to remember a hot dog, you visualize someone nailing a hot dog into your leg. Ouch! But it works. Because the human brain finds pain (physical and psychological) hard to forget. And guess what? You can also use this concept to craft "unforgettable" bullets:


- A secret "bear hug" defense that's so ruthless, there's a good chance he'll be pissing blood immediately afterwards.


- What many people do in their wills that could disinherit their children.


Bullet Point Secret #6: Straight Benefit


This is another simple and straight forward one - just state the claim (or more than one claim):


- How to hit the golf ball's "sweet spot" every time you swing the club. (Hit the ball in this exact spot and you almost can't help driving the ball farther and straighter down the fairway.)


- How to get your affiliate web pages featured on CNN, Google, Yahoo and other high traffic sites - 100% free.


Bullet Point Secret #7: Tell Them What Not to Do


This bullet is also related to curiosity. Except, you're not telling them what to do... you're telling them what not to do. This is especially powerful if it's something you know they are already doing. I love these bullets because people have an extremely hard time not buying if you do enough of them:


- The #1 worst mistake you can make with your wrist when swinging a golf club. (Even if you do everything else "right", your game will fall apart if you make this common mistake nobody talks about.)


- Four words that will destroy your chances of sponsoring new reps - even the ones who already want to sign up! (When I stopped saying these four words - that 99% of network marketers say during calls - my sponsoring rate shot through the roof.)


Bullet Point Secret #8: The Give Away


One thing you can do in your bullets is give away what the entire secret is. I don't recommend doing a lot of these. But tossing a few in the mix can ratchet up the "believability factor" of your other claims and promises (since you're demonstrating your knowledge):


- Nausea - settle a queasy stomach with a bowl of milk toast. Amazing! Page 5


- How to use simple cartoons to explode your sales… no matter what you sell or who you sell to. (For example, you'd be amazed at how easily a picture of a phone operator with her headset on can jack up your response!)


Bullet Point Secret #9: The 90% Solution


This is a great technique I learned from "A-list" copywriter David Deutsch. This is where you "bait the hook" 90% of the way, and purposely leave out that extra 10% the reader would need to get the whole secret. Here are some examples:


- Why you should never chew gum if you are trying to lose weight!


- The real reason why Prozac and Zoloft are so popular in this country! (Almost no one... not even doctors... understands the startling sexual implications.)


Bullet Point Secret #10: End with Dramatic Sub-headline


This a great way to break up the "sameness" of an ad with lots of bullets. What you do is turn the last six or seven words of your bullet into a dramatic sub-headline:


- How to "trick" copywriters who get paid hundreds of thousands of dollars per year… Into Doing All The "Dirty Work" For You!


Bullet Point Secret #11: Compressed Bullets


I first noticed this when reading a Gary Bencivenga ad. I tend to do these at the end of a long bullet list as a sort of "grand finale." And what you do is take a bunch of your short and "punchy" bullets and compress them into their own paragraph:


Why you do not want your press releases used word-for-word... How to use "sound bites" to make your press releases almost impossible for reporters to forget or ignore... Why you should almost never email your press releases... How to make yourself an instant "expert"... And much more.


Bullet Point Secret #12: Expansion Bullets


Finally, we have what I call "expansion" bullets. This is where you take a bullet and tack on a detailed explanation of it (using lots of tease). This is another great way to break up the "sameness" of the page when writing a fat list of bullets. Here's an example that also combines the compressed bullets, too:


- How to stop a life-threatening disease in your dog… before it surfaces.


Speaking of stopping diseases… chapter 32 of "Why You Should Never Give Your Dog A Bone" will show you…


How to Pay Little or Nothing for Your Dog's Vet Bills and Medications!


This is no joke.


In fact, if you want to kiss those sky-high vet bills and expensive pet medications goodbye forever… then turn immediately to page 92 and discover:


Why some colleges will perform veterinary services for your dog (as education for their veterinarian students) at a reduced cost (and sometimes free!)… A special website where you can buy dog medications for as much as 50% off… A secret way to get high-quality veterinary services 100% free for the rest of your dog's life… How to save a huge amount of money on your vet bills by asking your vet one (almost laughably simple) question… And more.


There are actually 12 money-saving secrets in this chapter.


And each one can save you a pile of money the next time your dog gets sick, hurt or just needs a routine checkup. Here are a few more of the little-known secrets revealed in this hot new book for dog owners:


More Bullet Point Secrets...


Here are some more bullet point "odds and ends":


- Beef up the "book ends": If you follow the formula we've been discussing in these "how to write a sales letter" issues, there's a very good chance your readers will at least skim the first few bullets... and then the last couple. Just like the P.S. is the second most read part of the ad, the last bullet point is the second most read bullet (usually). This is why you want your "book end" bullets to be the cream of the crop. It's also why (in many cases) I'll finish the bullets up with an expansion bullet, followed by a "compression" of short curiosity ones. Bam! Bam! Bam!


- Loop effect: If I have 30 or more bullets, I'll divide them into 2 or 3 sections of 10 (give or take). Then, I'll insert an expansion bullet that leads the reader back into more bullets (see the expansion bullet example above about the dog book). I won't say it's "hypnotic" (that term is way too overused). But it creates a sort of "loop" effect that tends to increase readership.


- Vary up your "ammo": This is important if you have lots of bullets in different categories in your ad (i.e. product bullets, bullets describing the product's author, more bullets in the P.S., etc). Instead of just "bullet" marks, you can use check marks, numbers, arrows and any other kind of mark. It helps keep things lively and unpredictable. You never want to bore anyone.


- Eye relief: Gary Halbert taught this all the time. What you want to do is bold every other bullet. That visually gives your bulleted list even more "eye relief."


- Cheat sheet: Want tons of great bullet ideas? Look ye at the table of contents in the highest selling books (the ones related to yours, of course). Read 'em and then "repurpose" those chapter titles, book titles and other ideas into your bullets.


- Practice makes perfect: Publicity "wizard" Paul Hartunian uses bullets in his press releases all the time (reporters love reading press releases with lots of hot bullets). And he once said he'll leave notes for people in "bullet" form just to keep practicing. Good advice.


- People to study: Here are some of my favorite bullet point writers. Just reading their bullets will give you a wold-class education on the subject: Gary Halbert, John Carlton, Mel Martin, Gary Bencivenga, Parris Lampropoulos, Ken McCarthy, Eugene Schwatz, Carline Anglade-Cole, Jim Rutz and Scott Haines. Also, check out magazines.com where you can see the latest magazine covers (you can get a lot of ideas from reading Cosmo's covers, for example, even if they are kinda raunchy). Yahoo and MSN also sometimes have good teaser bullets for their news stories, too.


So there you have it - a "baker's dozen" of ways to write bullets that almost force people to buy what you're selling.


Happy shooting!

### Questions from The Crypto Marketing Mail Bag


QUESTION: "You teach a lot about telling stories. But you don't seem to have a story in any of the ads for your own products."


It's about time someone noticed that. Yes, it's true, the 5 products I currently sell don't have stories in the ads. And the reason why is simple: I tell my "story" regularly in my daily emails. See, my business model is far more akin to talk radio than anything else. Take, for example, Rush Limbaugh. If Rush writes a book and he sells it to his own audience (and not via mass marketing, AdWords, space ads, etc), does he really need to tell his full story in the ad? Does he really need to "sell" the people who already know, like and trust him enough to tune in every day on his credibility? I say thee nay. He can immediately jump into the good stuff (the benefits/bullets) and safely skip the story part.


And that's how my business is set up, in a sense.


My ads are not intended to sell "cold" prospects (although I get a surprisingly large number of first time visitor sales - that I like to think is due to the sales letter bullets). Instead, they are written to those who tune into my "show" each day, are teased and tantalized (which I try to inject in each email) about a specific product, and want more. These customers are already pre-sold on me and just want to see what I got frying up for them in the kitchen. These are also the easiest kind of prospects to sell to. Heck, I don't even want cold prospects reading my ads much less buying (one reason I don't have affiliates). On the other hand... if I was selling to ice cold prospects... or if I was not having a daily dialogue with my list... or if I was selling to a mass market... I would approach my ads differently.


It's all about your unique strategy, strengths and market place dynamics.


There's no "one size fits all" business model (no matter what the goo-roos say).

### Critique Time


This month's critique "victim" is Stephen Davies. Steve's got a membership product about winning the lottery. This is an early draft of his ad (making it a perfect fit for a critique). You can read it at the link below, with my suggestions following:


http://www.CryptoMarketing.com/stuff/davies.pdf


Okay Steve, here are some ideas to consider:


- Test without the top graphic: For one thing, it screams "ad!" And secondly, you might be surprised how many marketers are finding their responses going up by removing their top graphics. It's worth a test if nothing else.


- Headline change: Try playing up the media directly in the headline (and not in its own box or anything like that). Mayhaps something like this:


Why Reuters, CBC and Hundreds Of Prestigious Media Outlets Worldwide are Talking About this Scientific New Way to Win The Lottery Almost Every Time You Play


- That's kind of rough (not great), but I think you get the idea. This way you can "borrow" the credibility from those media sources. Also, I'm kind of the lone voice in the wilderness about this, but I recommend not using the usual headline structures that scream "ad!" (like "revealed at last!", etc.). "How To" is fine, and any common book title structures are fine, but I'd steer clear of the obvious advertising phrases. You might also play around with using "NASA science" or something like that in your headline, too.


- Personal message: Instead of having it say "personal message from me" (who's "me"?). You may want to make it look more editorial. For example, just a picture of Kelly with something under it that provokes curiosity. Like, "Kelly Watson - Just an ordinary woman who figured out an extraordinary way to win the lottery almost every time." Doesn't have to be exactly like that. But just something to make people identify with her.


- Story/Mechanism: The story and mechanism for how it works are very well done, so good job on that! You also do a great job of connecting with the market (a lot of people feel the way Kelly does about money, their jobs, etc.)


- Disclaimer: I also like how you bring the reader "down" to a more realistic level and leave all the hype out - especially with that disclaimer about how you cannot guarantee their winnings, etc. A lot of sales letters skirt over this or try to hide it. When, in reality, revealing flaws helps response.


- Voice: It could just be me with this (others may disagree)... so take with a grain of salt... but just as a reader, I'm not convinced a female is actually talking in this sales letter. You might want to make sure it's in a woman's "voice." This is hard for most of us guys to do (I find it excruciatingly hard). But it's important to keep it in a woman's voice the whole time.


- Order buttons: You might test putting those order buttons farther up the page. Certainly one right after you introduce the product. The general idea is not to introduce an order link until value has been established. And since your membership is not super expensive, testing the order link farther up the page (starting after you introduce the membership - maybe right above the Q&A part) would be worth the effort.

## Chapter 5 -

## How to "Close the Deal" In Your Ads and Sales Letters


Dear Crypto Marketer,


We're finally at the end of our "how to write a sales letter" lesson. We've now covered nearly the entire "skeleton" - headline, lead, story, bullets... and today we're going to unearth the sales letter skeleton's final bone: The close. This is the part where you ask for the sale and get paid. Doing this right can often result in lots more of the green stuff. Below are some ways I do it in my ads. Including an idea you hardly ever see anyone else doing (but that works like gangbusters).


Ready?


Okay, then after the bullets what I like to do is jump right into the close. No dilly-dally, no messing around. Usually I do it with some kind of short, punchy connecting sentence like, "Anyway, here's the bottom line:" or "Bottom line?" or "Okay, so here's the deal..." Then I either give the price and tell them how to order, and/or jump into one (or all) of the following:


Close Component #1: The Guarantee


Contrary to popular goo-roo opinion, you don't always necessarily "need" a guarantee. In fact, in some markets (especially to a house list that loves you) it may have zero effect on your response. And if you know how to "posture" yourself (see below), a non-guarantee may even help your sales. You'll have to test it to see for sure, of course. But for now, here's a simple way to do a guarantee (feel free to "swipe", modify or adapt it as you see fit - just don't copy verbatim, that'd be lame):


Okay, so here's the deal:


I will send you Bullets, Bats and Blades to use and try for just $297.


That's a drop in the bucket compared to what the seminar attendees paid. Plus, if at any time in your life you don't think you can handle yourself and protect your family against some violent criminal (or gang of violent criminals) attacking you with a knife, bat or gun, return it for a full refund.


I don't care if you ask for a refund 10 years from now, either.


These DVD's must give you the peace of mind you can protect yourself and your family from the worst...


Or You Don't Pay for Them!


Here's how to order:


To have Bullets, Bats and Blades shipped right to your doorstep at my risk (not yours), just click the orange "Add to Cart" button below:


Close Component #2: Bonuses


Again, like with the guarantee, you do not always need bonuses. But I like to use them when possible and, if your bonuses target a strong emotion in your market, they can often carry the sales all by themselves! Now, there are tons of ways to do this. Below is one way I use a lot. Again, go ye forth and swipe, modify or adapt as you see fit. Also, please excuse the seemingly egotistical nature of this example. But I wrote this ad using the client's words (so it's not me "bragging" about myself to you):


EXCLUSIVE BONUS:


Interview with My Personal Copywriter


Last year, I held an awesome copywriting call series with my personal copywriter, Ben Settle. Ben is one of the single best copywriters I've ever seen, and during these calls we gave away some of the most incredible copywriting secrets we know including:


- A secret way of making money when sending email pitches... even if nobody buys from you.


- 11 ways to write auto-responder messages that drive in sales and leads.


- How to write ads that sell without using hype, lies or gimmicks.


- The "big secret" to writing profitable Google ads without struggle, frustration or pain.


- How to use your swipe file without breaking the law.


- How to get tens of thousands of dollars' worth of free "advertising" for little or no money.


- And a whole lot more


The price of this call series was $97.00 but it's yours free with The Copywriter's Guild today:


Note the bullets within the bonus copy? Those bullets are endlessly useful. And they make great "closers" when mentioning a bonus. Of course, you don't have to use bullets. You can also explain your bonuses with a short (and dramatic) presentation like this:


A few years ago Dan Kennedy noticed many of the highest-pulling ads ever created use the exact same "clicks of the dial" - special phrases, ideas, offers and themes that can super-charge any ad (no matter how "plain Jane") with an incredible amount of power and "sell."


When Ken used a few of these "clicks" in his own ads his response soared through the roof. He actually made so much money using these clicks... he paid someone to study hundreds of million-dollar ads and dig out every single click ever invented.


Turns out there are 26 of them.


And they're all contained in a secret list...


Now Worth a Fortune to the Few Who Possess It!


In fact, Ken has been offered thousands of dollars on the spot just to get a look at it.


And as insane as it sounds, Ken is giving this list away free with his copywriting course.


Notice how it used a story? Stories and bullets keep things interesting, dramatic and intriguing (curious). Very important - especially during the close.


Close Component #3: The Takeaway


This is probably one of the least used closes online (or offline). But it's often more powerful than guarantees, bonuses or anything else when done right. Why? I don't know. But there's something extremely persuasive about telling someone they either cannot have your product... or are not "qualified" to have it. I have been using this with maximum intensity lately and have not regretted it. Here are a few examples - go ahead and adapt them as you see fit (but again, don't copy my exact words, make your closes uniquely "yours"):


- This newsletter is not everyone's "cup of tea."


- I'm serious about this, too.


- For example, whiners and complainers who never take action (and need constant hand-holding) should not bother subscribing. Or those easily offended by my (occasional) right-wing politics. Or goo-roo fanboys always chasing the latest "magic pill" products that promise riches without effort.


- No offense, but if that's you, please do not waste your time.


I'm sure there's a place for you to learn from somewhere.


But It Ain't This Newsletter!


Here's another example (WARNING - This one goes right for the jugular, and I probably would not use it for most markets except this client's house list):


CC-SE is used by (and designed for) professional soldiers, athletes and trained killers who don't have time for gimmicks or nonsense.


These customers dare not miss a day and often order several bottles at a time to make sure they always have their "fix." (Which is one reason why we offer generous discounts on bulk orders.) So if you're a whiny little "gamma male" who wants a Band-Aid fix for your health, this product is not for you. You're better off hanging out with the women and children in the drug stores, and letting some faceless bureaucracy tell you what to do with your health.


But if you're a man who works hard, plays hard and wants a natural solution to age and injury related pain and health problems, then check out CC-SE today.


Especially while I'm still offering it to the civilian population at this inexpensive price.


Finally, here's a take-away that was ultimately not used by the client. Which (I would bet) probably cost them sales. I guess we'll never know, but here goes...


As reliable and profitable as Jim's course is, there is something you need to know: While most people can use Jim's system (even brand new MLM distributors or "technophobes"), there are some people who should not buy it, because they do not have the right "mindset" for AdWords.


And those are people who are cheap and lazy.


If you are cheap and lazy... then you should not buy Jim Yaghi's course or even have anything to do with AdWords. After all, you must be willing to spend at least "some" money to get started. And you must have at least "some" patience in order to follow Jim's system correctly.


Close Component #4: The Almighty PS:


I was always told the P.S. is the second most read part of a sales letter. However, I'm not so sure about this on the Internet. Whatever the case, it makes for a great sales tool. Sometimes I use it for testimonials. But I also use it for bonuses, to recap a deadline, insert a takeaway or all of the above. Test and experiment as you see fit. You can also use multiple PS's, too, there is no "rule" for them.


And that's it, my friend.


These last 5 issues (and the hot sheets that go with them) should get you off to a running start creating a profitable sales letter or ad. However, there is still much more you can learn to ratchet up your response (like testing, for example - which I'll probably do a future Crypto Marketing Newsletter issue on.)
